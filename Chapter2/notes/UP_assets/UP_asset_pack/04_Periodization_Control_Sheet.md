# Periodization Control Sheet

This sheet standardizes the historical sequence so each period does the same analytical work.

## Template logic

Each period should answer five questions:

1. What accumulation pattern dominates?
2. Which stagnation tendencies are most acute?
3. What is happening to class composition and struggle?
4. How does the state form respond?
5. Why does the period end?

---

## 1914-1932 — Rise of the PPNS

### Accumulation pattern
Export-economy destabilization, early import substitution, and institutional experimentation under the breakdown of liberal external articulation.

### Dominant stagnation tendencies
- rising organic composition of capital
- profit squeeze
- systemic volatility after export crisis

### Class dynamics
- intensification of labour unrest
- anti-oligarchic political opening
- revolutionary threat becomes legible to elites

### State-form response
- stronger executive state
- 1925 Constitution
- Central Bank and modernizing institutions
- reformism combined with containment

### Why the period ends
The Great Depression destroys the old export order and forces a more consolidated inward-looking developmental strategy.

### Drafting warning
Do not narrate this phase as pure institutional modernization. Its meaning lies in the combination of reform, containment, and crisis management.

---

## 1932-1947 — Consolidation of the PPNS

### Accumulation pattern
Inward-looking accumulation, consumer-goods ISI, state credit, and restoration of profitability.

### Dominant stagnation tendencies
- rising organic composition remains relevant
- underconsumption pressures persist beneath apparent recovery
- agrarian contradiction remains unresolved

### Class dynamics
- industrial workforce expands
- labour incorporated through patronage and cheap labour
- rural conflicts persist under repression

### State-form response
- stronger developmental coordination
- state-owned ventures and credit institutions
- selective inclusion and selective repression

### Why the period ends
Industrialization advances, but it does so on a disarticulated base that produces inflation, external vulnerability, and urban-rural imbalance.

### Drafting warning
This is consolidation, not stability. Keep the “non-stable” character visible.

---

## 1947-1957 — Resilience under dysfunctionality

### Accumulation pattern
Continued industrialization with deepening external constraint and inflationary disproportionality.

### Dominant stagnation tendencies
- profit squeeze pressures
- financial fragility
- external currency scarcity
- disproportionality rooted in agricultural stagnation and capital-goods dependence

### Class dynamics
- over-urbanization
- formation of urban marginal mass
- anti-communist exclusion plus popular reorganization

### State-form response
- stabilization attempts
- U.S.-advised monetary adjustment
- anti-communist containment
- no real resolution of structural bottlenecks

### Why the period ends
The unresolved tensions of industrialization, exclusion, and urban marginality reappear explosively in 1957.

### Drafting warning
This phase should be written as “stabilization without resolution.”

---

## 1957-1970 — Crisis of the PPNS

### Accumulation pattern
Expansion with rising popular incorporation, but deteriorating capital productivity and increasing developmental contradiction.

### Dominant stagnation tendencies
- underconsumption tendencies temporarily offset by wage-led utilization
- falling output-capital ratio
- rising political and organizational pressure on the regime

### Class dynamics
- re-legalization of the left
- peasant unionization
- urban squatter movement
- stronger working-class organization

### State-form response
- reform attempts intensify
- agrarian and political reforms widen expectations
- industrial patronage begins to crack

### Why the period ends
The development model cannot absorb the new level of popular empowerment without intensifying distributive and political conflict.

### Drafting warning
Do not write this period as linear radicalization. It is a contradictory interval of both reformist deepening and regime erosion.

---

## 1970-1973 — Socialist rupture amid twin crisis

### Accumulation pattern
High initial utilization and redistribution collide with structural bottlenecks, foreign-exchange pressure, sabotage, and global turbulence.

### Dominant stagnation tendencies
- disproportionality
- financial fragility
- profit squeeze
- realization crisis
- collapse of political reproducibility

### Class dynamics
- intensified popular participation
- workers’ control experiments
- open ruling-class confrontation
- military becomes decisive political actor

### State-form response
- accelerated nationalization and redistribution
- attempt to govern through mass participation and emergency management
- failure to restore stable reproduction

### Why the period ends
Domestic and global crisis tendencies become politically unmanageable, culminating in the coup as violent resolution.

### Drafting warning
This phase must not be reduced to “economic chaos.” It is the condensation point of the long crisis of the PPNS and the breakdown of global Fordism.

---

## Compression rule for later drafts

For each period, aim for:
- 1 paragraph on accumulation pattern,
- 1 paragraph on class dynamics,
- 1 paragraph on state-form response,
- 1 short closing sentence on transition to the next period.

That should keep the periodization sharp and comparable across sections.
