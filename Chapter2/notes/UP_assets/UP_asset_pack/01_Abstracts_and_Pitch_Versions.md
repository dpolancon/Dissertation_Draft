# Abstracts and Pitch Versions

## One-sentence thesis

The rise and fall of the Unidad Popular can be understood as the outcome of a twin crisis: the exhaustion of the Prebischian Populist National State in Chile and the simultaneous breakdown of global Fordism.

## 75-word pitch

This project explains the rise and fall of the Unidad Popular through the articulation of two crises: the crisis of the Prebischian Populist National State and the regime crisis of global Fordism. Using a variegated-capitalism framework and a periodization of Chile from 1914 to 1973, it argues that the Chilean road to socialism emerged not simply from political polarization, but from the erosion of a peripheral accumulation regime whose institutional capacities to offset crisis tendencies were increasingly exhausted.

## 150-word abstract

This paper revisits the political economy of the rise and fall of the Unidad Popular by locating it within a twin crisis. On the one hand, Chile’s Prebischian Populist National State progressively lost its capacity to offset stagnation tendencies associated with peripheral Fordism. On the other hand, the wider crisis of global Fordism reconfigured the international conditions under which national developmental projects could be reproduced. Drawing on a variegated-capitalism approach, the paper reconstructs the articulation between state form, accumulation regime, and class struggle across five periods from 1914 to 1973. It argues that the Unidad Popular did not emerge in an institutional vacuum, nor collapse solely because of domestic polarization or foreign intervention. Rather, it condensed a deeper contradiction between a crisis-ridden national developmental form and a world-historical restructuring of capitalism. The 1973 coup is therefore interpreted as both a political rupture and a violent resolution of an accumulation crisis.

## 250-word conference abstract

What explains the rise and fall of the Unidad Popular? This paper argues that the Chilean socialist experiment unfolded amid a twin crisis: the crisis of the Prebischian Populist National State (PPNS) and the regime crisis of global Fordism. Against interpretations that privilege either domestic political polarization or external intervention in isolation, the paper reconstructs the Unidad Popular as the political condensation of a deeper contradiction in capitalist development.

The argument draws on a variegated-capitalism framework, understood as a Marxist critique of methodological nationalism and static institutional-complementarity approaches. Instead of treating Chile as a self-contained national case, the paper situates it within the uneven and relational dynamics of the capitalist world system. It conceptualizes the PPNS as a developmental and crisis-managing state form linked to import-substitution industrialization, structural heterogeneity, and the management of external constraint under peripheral Fordism.

Empirically, the paper proposes a periodization of Chilean accumulation from 1914 to 1973, identifying the varying capacity of the PPNS to offset stagnation tendencies associated with profitability, realization, and external reproduction. This periodization traces the rise, consolidation, resilience, and eventual crisis of the PPNS, culminating in the Unidad Popular period, when domestic crisis tendencies intersected with the collapse of Bretton Woods, intensifying external constraint, ruling-class sabotage, and open class struggle.

The paper concludes that the coup of 1973 should be read not only as a political overthrow, but as the violent resolution of a crisis of accumulation, state form, and world-historical capitalist restructuring. In that sense, the Chilean road to socialism appears as both a national political process and a strategically situated episode in the global crisis of Fordism.
