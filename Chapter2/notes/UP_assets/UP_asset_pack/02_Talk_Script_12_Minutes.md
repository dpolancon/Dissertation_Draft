# Talk Script — 12 Minutes

## Opening

Good morning. My paper asks a simple but loaded question: what explains the rise and fall of the Unidad Popular?

My answer is that the Chilean socialist experiment unfolded amid a twin crisis. The first was the crisis of a specific Latin American developmental state form, which I call the Prebischian Populist National State, or PPNS. The second was the wider crisis of global Fordism. My argument is that we understand the Unidad Popular better when we see it not as an isolated national anomaly, but as a politically explosive articulation of those two crises.

## Why this matters

This matters for at least three reasons.

First, Chile became one of the decisive historical tests of the democratic road to socialism.

Second, 1973 occupies a central place in the genealogy of neoliberal restructuring.

Third, the case forces us to think relationally. It is not enough to choose between internal class conflict and external pressure. The more interesting question is how domestic contradictions were mediated through a changing world order.

## Framework

The paper uses a variegated-capitalism approach, but in a distinctly Marxist register.

What I take from that literature is not the idea of stable institutional complementarity. It is the idea that different institutional ensembles have different capacities to offset crisis tendencies. So the question becomes: under what conditions could the Chilean developmental state reproduce accumulation, contain class conflict, and manage external constraint? And at what point did that capacity begin to fail?

I use the concept of the PPNS to name that state form. It is the state form associated with import-substitution industrialization, with the management of external vulnerability, and with a populist developmental coalition under conditions of structural heterogeneity.

## Core conceptual move

The key conceptual move is to place the PPNS within peripheral Fordism.

In core Fordism, accumulation was organized through mass production, mass consumption, relatively strong labour incorporation, and a Keynesian welfare state under a world regime of capital and trade controls.

In peripheral Fordism, these features were reworked under different conditions: weaker internal integration, stronger external constraint, imported capital goods, industrial patronage, and a developmental state trying to compensate for structural bottlenecks.

So the issue is not whether Chile had a defective copy of core Fordism. The issue is whether this peripheral configuration could offset the stagnation tendencies generated by its own uneven reproduction.

## Analytical framework

To answer that, I classify stagnation tendencies into two broad families.

The first is deficit of surplus value: rising organic composition of capital and profit squeeze.

The second is excess of surplus value: underconsumption and financial fragility.

The broader claim is that an accumulation regime can be evaluated in terms of its capacity to offset these tendencies. The PPNS did not simply govern. It regulated, displaced, and sometimes intensified crisis tendencies.

## Periodization

The empirical part of the paper periodizes Chile from 1914 to 1973 in five phases.

From 1914 to 1932, we have the rise of the PPNS under the increasing dysfunctionality of liberal capitalism. The external shock of World War I, the crisis of the export economy, and the erosion of the oligarchic republic created the conditions for a stronger interventionist state.

From 1932 to 1947, we see consolidation. Import-substitution industrialization, state credit, and inward-looking accumulation helped restore profitability, but the regime remained weakly coherent because it relied on surplus labour, industrial patronage, and unresolved agrarian contradictions.

From 1947 to 1957, the PPNS shows resilience, but only under persistent dysfunctionality. Industrialization deepens, yet agriculture stagnates, inflation rises, foreign-exchange pressures intensify, and over-urbanization produces a growing urban marginal mass.

From 1957 to 1970, the PPNS enters crisis. Popular mobilization expands, labour power rises, agrarian reform and peasant unionization advance, and industrial patronage starts to break down. Capacity utilization increases, but capital productivity deteriorates and the development model becomes increasingly fragile.

Finally, from 1970 to 1973, the crisis of the PPNS converges with the crisis of global Fordism. The collapse of Bretton Woods, worsening external constraint, domestic sabotage, inflationary disproportionality, and open class struggle all intensify. The Unidad Popular becomes the site where these contradictions are condensed.

## Historical payoff

This reading changes how we interpret the coup.

The coup was not simply the result of polarization, nor only of U.S. intervention, nor only of the strategic errors of the left. It was also the violent resolution of a crisis of accumulation. In other words, a developmental state form had reached the limits of its capacity to reproduce capitalist order, and the socialist road emerged precisely at the moment when those limits were becoming unmanageable.

## Closing

So the central takeaway is this: the rise and fall of the Unidad Popular can be understood only if we place Chilean class struggle within the historical articulation of a peripheral developmental state crisis and a world-historical crisis of Fordism.

That is what gives 1973 its significance. It was not only a Chilean rupture. It was a strategically situated episode in the global restructuring of capitalism.

Thank you.

## Optional 90-second Q&A backup lines

### If asked “Why not just dependency theory?”

Because dependency theory captures external asymmetry well, but it is less useful for specifying how crisis tendencies are mediated through state form, institutional coherence, and accumulation-regime dynamics.

### If asked “What is new here?”

The novelty is the articulation: the paper links the national developmental state crisis to the world crisis of Fordism and uses that articulation to periodize Chilean accumulation before and during the Unidad Popular.

### If asked “What is the empirical anchor?”

The empirical anchor is the periodized assessment of how the PPNS offset or failed to offset stagnation tendencies tied to profitability, realization, and external reproduction.
