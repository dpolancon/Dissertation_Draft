## Research question

What explains the rise and fall of the Unidad Popular?

## Why this matters

The problems of economic development somewhere are a matter of interest everywhere, particularly when they culminate in socialist rupture (Gerschenkron, 1962). In that sense, the thousand days of Allende's government constituted a nodal conjuncture of world history that shaped the trajectory of class struggle well beyond Chile.

This conjuncture matters for at least five reasons:

- it became part of the broader problem of the democratic road to socialism;
- it fed debates around the Historical Compromise in Italy (Santoni, 2014; Magri, 2019);
- it reinforced the perceived impossibility of a democratic road to socialism in Latin America, strengthening armed-struggle strategies in countries such as Peru and Colombia;
- it marked a major point of departure for the neoliberal counterrevolution (Klein, 2007; Harvey, 2007; Fischer, 2009);
- it anticipated the rise of neoliberal governmentality and its later constitutionalization under the Pinochet dictatorship, while also illustrating the multipolar production of capitalist modernity through South-South diffusion of neoliberal economic thought (Rupprecht, 2016; 2020).

## Core argument

My core argument is that the rise and fall of the Chilean socialist revolution took place amid a twin crisis:

1. the crisis of the **Prebischian Populist National State (PPNS)**; and
2. the regime crisis of **global Fordism**.

The Unidad Popular was therefore not simply the outcome of domestic political polarization, nor merely the result of external intervention. It emerged and collapsed within the articulation of a specifically Latin American state form with a wider world-historical restructuring of capitalism.

## Theoretical framework

### Variegated Capitalism as comparative political economy

This project draws on a Variegated Capitalism approach (Peck and Theodore, 2007), understood as a Marxist critique of standard varieties-of-capitalism approaches.

The framework is useful for three reasons:

- it criticizes methodological nationalism and the neoclassical residues of conventional comparative institutionalism;
- it shifts attention from institutional complementarity to **institutional coherence**, that is, to the capacity of institutional ensembles to offset crisis tendencies or, conversely, to reproduce dysfunctionality (Vidal, 2014);
- it grants primacy to the capitalist world system while leaving concrete causal mediation to the strategic selectivity of the state, or what Jessop (2015) describes as the ecological dominance of the capital relation.

### Intended contribution to the framework

The intended contribution is to extend this framework to Latin American social formations.

More specifically, the argument proceeds in four steps:

- it treats Latin American trajectories relationally rather than through a self-enclosed national or dependency-only optic;
- it identifies national accumulation regimes through the articulation of a specific developmental state form, the **Prebischian Populist National State (PPNS)**, with the wider global accumulation regime of Fordism and post-Fordism;
- it asks whether peripheral Fordism and its corresponding state form could offset capitalist stagnation tendencies;
- it proposes that the demise of the PPNS is the key locus through which the variegated neoliberalization of Latin American social formations can be explained.

## Conceptual architecture

### Fordism

Marx identified successive stages of the labour process under capitalism: cooperation, manufacture, and large-scale industry. Fordism refers to the mature stage associated with large-scale industry and, historically, with the mid-twentieth-century organization of capitalist growth.

In broad terms, a Fordist accumulation regime can be defined by the following features:

- a core employment sector in mass-production oligopolistic manufacturing;
- mass consumption supported by high unionization and the expansion of the domestic market;
- producer-led national supply chains;
- a financially autonomous Keynesian national welfare state operating under an international regime of trade and capital controls.

### Peripheral Fordism

Previous regulationist work identified peripheral Fordism as the penetration of Fordist forms of production into economies whose growth depended less on mass consumption than on export-oriented articulation. Lipietz's formulation is important here: peripheral Fordism exists only when growth in the home market for manufactured goods plays a real role in the national regime of accumulation.

That formulation is useful but ultimately insufficient because it remains tied to a methodological nationalist view. This project therefore reconstructs peripheral Fordism in relational terms, as a historically specific articulation of national institutional forms with the uneven dynamics of the capitalist world system.

### The PPNS

Within that relational reconstruction, the distinctive Latin American state form is the **Prebischian Populist National State (PPNS)**. The PPNS is conceived as a developmental and crisis-managing state form linked to import-substitution industrialization, the management of external constraint, and the attempt to stabilize peripheral capitalist reproduction under conditions of structural heterogeneity.

## Analytical framework: institutional capacities to offset crisis tendencies

For Marxist political economy, capitalism is an inherently crisis-prone system of social production and reproduction. Crises are endogenous to the normal functioning of the system itself (Basu, 2019). Yet capitalism also develops institutional capacities that can offset crisis tendencies, resolve partial crises, and enable restructuring processes that preserve dominant class rule (Vidal, 2019).

From that standpoint, accumulation regimes can be evaluated in terms of their **functionality** or **dysfunctionality**, that is, in terms of their capacity to offset specific stagnation tendencies.

Following Basu's (2019) typology, two broad classes of stagnation tendencies can be distinguished.

### 1. Deficit of surplus value

These are tendencies in which profitability declines even though the profit rate remains around its normal level because surplus value becomes insufficient.

This category includes:

- **Rising organic composition of capital**, referring to the relation between profitability and technological change;
- **Profit squeeze**, referring to the relation between profitability and distributive conflict, particularly when labour strength compresses profits.

### 2. Excess of surplus value

These are tendencies in which produced surplus value cannot be fully realized because effective demand is chronically insufficient, so commodities are sold below their prices of production and the realized profit rate falls below its normal level.

This category includes:

- **Underconsumption**, when effective demand falls short of potential output;
- **Financial fragility**, when the financial system loses its capacity to channel surplus value into expenditure streams or when firms are unable to convert profits in domestic currency into foreign exchange needed for capital-goods imports.

### Regime functionality

Because each stagnation tendency generates fragility, instability, and reproduction problems, an accumulation regime can be assessed according to the extent to which it can offset or contain them. Here it is useful to reserve the word **crisis** for a deeper structural interruption of the economy-wide circuit of capital, while treating the other mechanisms as **stagnation tendencies** that may accumulate into crisis.

### Empirical operationalization

To operationalize the framework empirically, the analysis tracks the reproductive patterns of profitability from the outbreak of World War I, where the origins of the PPNS can be traced, through its demise in 1973.

Weisskopf's (1979) profitability framework provides the methodological bridge. It allows the reconstruction of macro-historical patterns of stability and instability by examining how aggregate demand, distribution, and technological change reproduce or undermine profit-oriented, market-mediated capital accumulation.

## Historical periodization

## 1914-1932 — Rise of the PPNS under increasing dysfunctionality of liberal capitalism

The rise of the PPNS in Chile can be traced to the explosion of World War I. With an already developed industrial base, Chilean elites responded to the collapse of British imports by engaging in import-substitution policies, promoting infant industry through tariffs and by building institutions that supported capitalist development, such as the Central Bank and mining and industrial credit institutions (Palma, 1979).

This period also saw the demise of the oligarchic republic, coupled with the resilience of dominant groups that conceded constitutional reform in 1925 while attempting to contain the communist threat (Rasmussen et al., 2023).

Finally, the nitrate crisis that began in 1919 and deepened after the Great Depression generated unemployment on a massive scale, pushing the expansion of social insurance and emergency institutional responses.

### Key dynamics

- collapse of liberal-capitalist external articulation;
- early ISI under elite control;
- institutional emergence of the PPNS;
- combined economic dislocation and political containment.

## 1932-1947 — Consolidation of the PPNS after the Great Depression

Profitability was restored through inward-looking capital accumulation, intensive import-substitution industrialization, especially in consumer goods, the strengthening of credit institutions, and the emergence of state-owned industrial ventures.

Labour relations during this phase were shaped by **industrial patronage**, sustained by cheap labour under conditions of rural-urban transition, demographic change, and an effectively unlimited labour supply.

Yet the coherence of the accumulation regime remained weak. Rapid industrialization relied heavily on surplus labour, while agrarian stagnation and rural exclusion intensified social conflict in the countryside. Demands for agrarian reform and peasant unionization were met largely through repression and, at points, the illegalization of the Communist Party.

### Key dynamics

- restoration of profitability through inward-looking accumulation;
- rapid but unstable industrialization;
- emergence of industrial patronage;
- unresolved agrarian contradiction beneath urban-industrial growth.

## 1947-1957 — Resilience of the PPNS: stabilization under persistent dysfunctionality

This phase was marked by a partial crisis of disproportionality. Rapid industrialization, combined with stagnation in agriculture, generated inflationary pressures and scarcity of international currency.

In response, the state moved toward a primitive form of monetary stabilization under U.S. policy advice, most notably the Klein-Saks mission. At the same time, the relationship between the Catholic University and the University of Chicago laid the groundwork for the formation of the Chicago Boys.

The continuous but disarticulated process of industrialization, combined with over-urbanization, also produced an urban marginal mass that could not be absorbed by the industrial sector. It is in this context that the modern squatter movement emerged, including the 1957 land seizure at La Victoria in Santiago.

This was also a period in which consumer-goods industrialization deepened dependence on imported capital goods, worsening the external constraint. At the level of the labour process, a primitive Taylorism appears to have set in motion a form of Marx-biased technical change.

### Key dynamics

- inflationary disproportionality and external constraint;
- early stabilization under U.S. advisory influence;
- over-urbanization and marginality;
- mounting dependence on imported capital goods.

## 1957-1970 — Crisis of the PPNS and the erosion of liberal-democratic stability

This phase marks the high dysfunctionality of the PPNS and the transition toward a crisis of liberal democracy.

The re-legalization of the Communist Party and Allende's first presidential candidacy in 1958 signaled a new conjuncture. Urban popular mobilization expanded through a dynamic squatter movement, while labour militancy intensified in the context of rising class solidarity and a relative depletion of reserve labour.

The period also appears to have benefited, at least provisionally, from favourable terms of trade linked to military Keynesian expansion in the United States and rising demand for copper, though this remains a working hypothesis.

At the same time, consecutive agrarian reforms and peasant unionization increased working-class power. Capacity utilization rose in a wage-led fashion, but the output-capital ratio fell as capital-intensive techniques expanded faster than labour productivity. The result was a deepening contradiction within the development model itself, visible as an emergent crisis of industrial patronage and a failed process of copper "Chileanization."

### Key dynamics

- rising labour and popular-class empowerment;
- stronger peasant organization and urban mobilization;
- wage-led utilization gains alongside falling capital productivity;
- erosion of industrial patronage and mounting regime crisis.

## 1970-1973 — Failed socialist revolution amid the crisis of global Fordism

The Unidad Popular period condensed both the crisis of the PPNS and the broader crisis of global Fordism.

### 1970-1971

The electoral triumph of the Unidad Popular triggered an immediate financial crisis (Girardi and Bowles, 2018). Popular mobilization surged, including an explosion of the squatter movement (Murphy).

The government launched a Keynesian stimulus intended to secure full employment and raise capacity utilization. This succeeded in increasing demand, but it also generated consumption-production imbalances because of structural bottlenecks in agriculture and transportation.

At the same time, the social property area of the economy was advanced through copper nationalization and the nationalization of the banking sector. High utilization strengthened working-class power and enabled experiments in shop-floor democracy that challenged industrial patronage as a form of management (Zimbalist).

#### Key dynamics

- immediate financial destabilization after UP victory;
- expansionary policy and rising utilization;
- acceleration of nationalization;
- strengthening of workers' control and popular participation.

### 1971-1972

The initial expansion gave way to a crisis of disproportionality expressed in galloping inflation. Structural bottlenecks intensified as imports of spare parts from the United States became increasingly difficult.

External pressure deepened the crisis. Harassment of Chilean copper exports and operations abroad obstructed realization, while the world context shifted dramatically with the collapse of Bretton Woods in August 1971, signaling the crisis of Atlantic Fordism.

At the domestic level, the terms of trade deteriorated, ruling-class sabotage intensified, and the 1972 bosses' lockout disrupted supply chains. At the same time, the popular movement deepened its support for the Unidad Popular through growing participation in price control and distribution.

#### Key dynamics

- inflationary disproportionality;
- intensifying external constraint;
- collapse of Bretton Woods as global turning point;
- domestic sabotage and expanded popular mobilization.

### 1973

By 1973, structural bottlenecks in agriculture and transportation, combined with foreign-exchange scarcity and deteriorating terms of trade, had generated a severe crisis of disproportionality and hyperinflation.

Class struggle became fully open and intensified. The wider international crisis also sharpened, with the OPEC shock helping to put the final nail in the coffin of Fordist accumulation.

Profit squeeze, disproportionality, and financial vulnerability articulated into a deeper structural crisis of capital, driving a collapse of capacity utilization and a severe underconsumption problem. The incorporation of the military into the Unidad Popular government failed to stabilize the situation politically. In the end, the impossibility of political stabilization amid intense class struggle culminated in Pinochet's coup.

#### Key dynamics

- full articulation of domestic and global crisis;
- collapse of political stabilization;
- crisis of realization, profitability, and external reproduction;
- military coup as violent resolution of the impasse.

---

## Provisional conclusion

The rise and fall of the Unidad Popular can be understood as the political expression of a deeper structural contradiction. The Chilean socialist road emerged when the PPNS was already losing its capacity to manage the stagnation tendencies of peripheral Fordism, and it collapsed as that national crisis converged with the broader breakdown of global Fordism.

In this reading, the coup of 1973 was not merely a political rupture. It was also the violent resolution of a crisis of accumulation, state form, and world-historical capitalist restructuring.
