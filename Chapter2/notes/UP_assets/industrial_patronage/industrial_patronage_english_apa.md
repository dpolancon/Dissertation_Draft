# Industrial Patronage and Socialist Revolution: The Labor Process in Developmentalist Chile

*Diego Polanco*

## Introduction

A distinctive feature of Marxist political economy is that it was a pioneer in the study of the labor process. This means that, when approaching phenomena such as wage determination and workers' labor conditions, it does not look only at market dynamics. It also seeks to analyze the control capital exercises over labor in the productive process, as well as the forms of domination that condition the reproduction of labor power in the broadest sense: in the workplace, in the household and family, and in institutions that function as ideological state apparatuses and as mechanisms of social reproduction for society as a whole, such as school and health systems.

In the case of a social formation on the periphery of world capitalism such as Chile, it is necessary to consider the relative position of the labor process at the national scale in relation to the labor process at the center of the capitalist world-system, since it is in those geographies that the innovations advancing the technological frontier are developed and from which capital goods are supplied to peripheral economies. This article characterizes the labor process in Chile during the developmentalist period from a comparative and relational perspective with the Fordist labor process in the United States and the broader North Atlantic sphere, while also placing it in historical perspective with Chile's own trajectories of modernization. In this way, industrialization in Chile during the period usually known as developmentalist can be understood as a process of modernization truncated by the limitations of the Chilean capitalist class itself.

The rise of Popular Unity to executive state power, together with the implementation of its program of transition to socialism "from above," as well as the experiences of participation in and control over the labor process within---and beyond---the social area of the economy exercised by the working class "from below," can be understood as a process that emerged from the very crisis of the labor institutions that governed the country during the developmentalist period. The democratizing revolution in the social relations of production that erupted during the Popular Unity government can therefore be understood as a popular and emancipatory project of modernization, alternative to capitalist modernization.

The aim, then, is to show that the rise and fall of the patronal institutional order governing labor relations in developmentalist Chile confirms the old maxim of historical materialism: the social relations that enable the development of the productive forces eventually become their own fetters. Put differently, the social conditions that make a regime of accumulation possible are the very conditions that generate its self-destruction.

## North Atlantic Fordism: A Regime of Accumulation on a Global Scale

The beginning of the twentieth century was marked by intense economic turbulence and the end of an era defined by the decline of liberal forms of economic regulation and the erosion of British hegemony in global capitalism. If the First World War had already allowed the United States to climb within the hierarchies of the interstate system in the geopolitical struggle to lead world capitalist development, the economic restructuring that followed the Second World War subordinated European nations beneath its financial umbrella, establishing the United States as the hegemonic imperial power of the West.[^1]

Yet the Americanization of the world economy was not defined only by the military and financial hegemony of the U.S. empire. It also took the form of a mode of production oriented toward manufacturing and large-scale output, toward relatively closed domestic markets, toward low-intensity oligopolistic competition, and toward inclusive institutions established by the Keynesian national welfare state. This productive and institutional assemblage enabled and promoted working-class unionization and the provision of social rights, thereby configuring a new scale of capitalist development by incorporating a new element: mass consumption.[^2] In this way, U.S. capitalism led not only a new organization of the capitalist mode of production on a world scale under the shelter of its financial hegemony, but also a specific historical form of capitalist production and reproduction during this period: Fordism.

With varying degrees of success[^3], European nations developed their own ways of adapting to the hegemonic regime of accumulation of the North Atlantic sphere, replicating the essential institutional axes of that regime with one variation that would lay the basis for the development of the old continent: an orientation toward the internal market, but now at the continental scale through the constitution of the European Common Market. European nations thus maintained economies open to one another, promoting international trade and the technological transfers associated with it among European states, while constituting a relatively closed economy at the continental level.[^4] Smaller economies such as Canada on the American side, or Austria, Denmark, and Sweden on the European side, also achieved mass consumption through strategic insertion into the international division of labor, whose transatlantic dynamics were decisively shaped by the main Fordist sectors of the hegemonic social formations of the regime.[^5]

This gave rise to a new era of imperialism, marking a fundamental difference from what Lenin correctly identified as the causes of the First World War.[^6] In the liberal phase of capitalism, the development of large-scale industry culminated in a succession of world wars because of two aspects of the same historical process. On the one hand, there was the tendency toward the integration of different fractions of capital---industrial, commercial, and financial---under the command of finance, banking, and the state; on the other, there was the inability to resolve internally the tendencies toward overaccumulation, which pushed states toward the conquest of markets and territories for commodity realization and strategic control over raw materials. By contrast, in the Fordist phase of capitalism, the class compromise between workers and capitalists promoted by the Keynesian state enabled the crisis tendencies of capitalist economies to be provisionally resolved through mass consumption.

The conditions of imperialism in the Fordist period were therefore no longer defined primarily by competition among states seeking to expand their markets, but rather by a transnational historical bloc constituted through national compacts among industrial capitalists, unionized workers, and Keynesian-oriented state bureaucracies, internationally organized through the autonomous policy space allowed by the Bretton Woods financial architecture under U.S. financial hegemony.[^7] Consequently, the imperial interests of the North Atlantic Fordist regime of accumulation were focused mainly on the strategic control of raw materials, which was in turn intimately linked to Cold War dynamics and the West's need to contain the "communist threat," defend the legitimacy of liberal democracy, and impose capitalism as the only possible path of development.

## Industrial Patronage: Chilean Adaptation to North Atlantic Fordism

The Great Depression of 1929 set in motion a global process of capitalist restructuring that would be completed only after the end of the Second World War. Yet the retreat of capital internationalization and the establishment of new trading patterns had already begun with the outbreak of the First World War in 1914, which in Latin America took the form of the withdrawal of British financial capital and imports. The Wall Street crash accelerated that process, intensifying the decline of commercial integration between Latin America and the advanced capitalist countries, a decline that would collapse definitively with the outbreak of the Second World War, as shown by the dramatic fall in the region's exports of goods and services from around 20% in the 1930s to levels near or below 10% in the 1940s and 1950s.[^8]

This process of marginalizing the economies of our region constituted a new form of dependence for Latin America,[^9] one that restored capitalist profitability,[^10] restarted capital accumulation, and shifted economic policy toward protectionism by constructing an institutional assemblage aimed at sustaining inward-oriented industrialization designed to produce previously imported goods.[^11] In this way, a new form of dependence on the North Atlantic Fordist regime of accumulation emerged---one no longer determined mainly by demand for primary-export goods, but by the articulation of technological and financial dependence around import-substitution industrialization. Although this process oriented economic development toward the domestic market, it did so under a necessary demand for imported capital goods---machinery and equipment that required foreign currency---thereby imposing a specifically financial dependency.[^12]

Latin America's peripheral condition within the global hegemony of the Fordist regime sedimented institutional logics across different social axes such as the productive apparatus, forms of competition, and the state,[^13] logics that took specific forms in the region's national social formations. For the Chilean developmentalist period---specifically from 1930 to 1970, when its crisis would open the way to socialist revolution---I propose the category of industrial patronage. This denomination is grounded in the historical formation of Chile's ruling class, in its oligarchic and patronal character, and in landlords' control over peasant labor under hacienda relations: inquilinaje, a system of land tenure and agrarian production that emerged alongside capitalist development in Chile but does not properly correspond to fully capitalist social relations of production.[^14] Three features of inquilinaje are worth emphasizing.[^15] First, the landed elite treated peasant labor power as a labor cost in order to maximize productivity per unit of land; together with the existence of a reserve labor army,[^16] the mobility of rural labor, and the early commercial integration of Chilean agriculture into world trade,[^17] this reveals an institutional logic with capitalist traits. Second, land ownership was absolutely concentrated in landed property, whose holders exercised undisputed sovereignty over peasant plots. Third, there was no legal order positioning peasants and landlords as formally equal before the law; noncompliance could be punished through imprisonment and forced labor, which makes the sanctioning system of inquilinaje qualitatively distinct from a capitalist one. The intersection of capitalist and non-capitalist institutional logics allows us to argue for the pseudo-capitalist character of Chile's agrarian relations from the postcolonial period up to the rise of industrial patronage as the dominant institutional form of labor relations from the Great Depression to the military coup.[^18]

The link between inquilinaje and industrial patronage lies in the integrated character of the landed elite and the industrial capitalist class as a ruling class, through the development of national banking and their integration in the second half of the nineteenth century into the circuits of British imperialism through transnational finance capital, which became a historical bloc in Chile during the nitrate cycle extending from the aftermath of the War of the Pacific to the outbreak of the First World War. This means that no capitalist class autonomous from landowning classes existed in Chile in the way it did in Europe's path to capitalism,[^19] and therefore the direct control of the ruling classes over the labor process was marked by continuity with the patronal logics of inquilinaje, displaced in space and time from the countryside to the urban settings where extractive and manufacturing industries were established.

Before 1930, Chile's population was predominantly rural, and labor power was largely subsumed under the institutional logic of inquilinaje. The nitrate boom from the late nineteenth century onward set in motion a process of industrialization through the reinvestment of nitrate rents into productive capital, giving rise to industrial patronage as a continuation of the patronal logic of inquilinaje in urban spaces, particularly in extractive and manufacturing industries.[^20] Three characteristics stand out. First, management was anchored in kinship relations among owners, so control over the labor process was exercised not only through capitalist authority but through family identity itself. Second, housing and recreational infrastructure were provided to workers in areas adjacent to the workplace, allowing employers to discipline labor not only through employment and wages but also within the sphere of working-class social reproduction and family life. Third, the Constitution of 1925 and the developmental role of the state from the 1930s onward added a new feature: the promotion of a trade unionism subservient to the capitalist employer, based on patronage, favors, and clientelistic ties.[^21]

The urbanization driven by the development of industry and services during the nitrate cycle, together with the expansion of the public sector and the consequent growth of the middle classes, enabled Chilean society to absorb modern scientific knowledge to a greater degree. In a context of political turbulence, and in the ruling class's effort to contain popular radicalization, this led to the rise and expansion of fiscal spending,[^22] particularly in health care and social security. The effects on public health were profound: infant mortality fell dramatically, raising life expectancy from roughly 30 to 60 years between 1920 and 1970 and thus generating a major demographic shift. After 1930, Chile's population surpassed 4 million in 1940, 5 million in 1952, 7 million in 1960, and nearly 10 million in 1970, while the rural population remained stagnant at around 2 million throughout the period.[^23] Accelerated urbanization therefore imposed a secular retreat of inquilinaje as the dominant logic of labor relations, replacing it with industrial patronage as the central institutional axis through which the ruling class controlled the labor process in Chile.

It can be argued that industrial capital---and especially manufacturing capital, which benefited from the tariff protection of import-substitution industrialization---failed to incorporate a majority segment of the labor force under its direct control, while a growing share of the working class remained excluded from wage labor and was relegated to reproducing its livelihood through informal work.[^24][^25] Yet this overlooks the wage relation and its spatial dimensions linking non-wage labor to wage labor. In wage terms, the point is that industrial wages were kept under downward pressure by the role of the industrial reserve army composed of the unemployed, informal workers, and the peasantry. Spatially, the same relation appeared in the tendency of migrants from countryside to city, or marginalized sectors living in indigence, to occupy land near industrial neighborhoods because of the gravitational pull of these territories in search of jobs either in industry or in the provision of informal services.[^26] Industrial patronage is therefore an appropriate concept for describing the dominant institutional logic of the labor process in both a narrow and a broader sense, since it also includes mechanisms for controlling labor power in the sphere of social reproduction, where the boundaries of the right to the city were contested.[^27]

## Crisis of Industrial Patronage and Socialist Revolution

In the preface to A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy,[^28] Marx argues that social relations of production initially enable the development of the productive forces, but that once a certain stage is reached, they enter into contradiction with them, so that the very same relations of production become fetters on the further development of the productive forces. More than 150 years later, this old maxim of historical materialism still offers a fundamental tool for understanding social transformation because it emphasizes the emergent properties of institutional regimes: the very conditions that allow them to be established are those that provoke their collapse and self-destruction. Institutions, in other words, have endogenous tendencies toward crisis.

In the case of industrial patronage as the dominant institutional logic of labor relations in developmentalist Chile, this dynamic can be identified in the growth of a social wage. The health-care and educational institutions that emerged under the expansion of public spending extended life expectancy through the control of infant mortality discussed above, while also instructing, disciplining, and complexifying labor power through the expansion of the school system.[^29] Between 1938 and 1973, the Chilean state expanded school enrollment to 100% at the primary level, 23.25% at the secondary level, and 3.13% at the tertiary level, thanks to an accelerated pace of expansion at all three levels unlike anything previously seen in the country's history; this translated into a decline in illiteracy from 46.7% in 1930 to 12.8% in 1970.[^30] While the establishment of a public health system produced demographic change and accelerated urbanization created an unlimited supply of labor that kept industrial wages down, the expansion of schooling generated the conditions for challenging industrial patronage. If education in capitalist societies certainly helps form and regulate the flow of labor power into the labor market, educational modernization also provides the conditions for the emancipation of the oppressed by expanding cognitive capacities, democratizing access to knowledge critical of the established order, and eroding the ruling class's traditional values.[^31]

The tendency toward crisis in patronage as the dominant institutional logic of labor relations was also expressed in a secular growth of support for progressive and revolutionary parties, a process made possible, of course, by the expansion of suffrage. From 1930 onward, the ratio of voters to the voting-age population rose rapidly, from roughly 15% in 1932 to close to 75% in the 1973 parliamentary elections, owing to the extension of women's suffrage in 1952, the modernization of the electoral system that prevented landlords from transporting inquilinos to vote, and the enfranchisement of illiterate voters in 1972.[^32] Between 1937 and 1969, the electoral register grew by more than 1.8 million new voters. Center parties---the Radical Party, Agrarian Labor Party, and Christian Democrats---captured 38.1% of the preferences of these new voters, while the parties of the left---the Communist and Socialist Parties---won 38.8%, leaving the right with only 15.2%. The intense social mobilization and repressive response of the Christian Democrats during the Frei Montalva government would break this tendency and radicalize political preferences in favor of the left. With the entry of more than 1.3 million new voters in the 1969 and 1973 elections, the parties now organized in Popular Unity captured 40.3%, while the center and the right obtained 22% and 23.5%, respectively.

This process of radicalization took place in a broader context of popular empowerment during the long sixties (1957-1970), whose conditions of possibility lay in the exhaustion of the unlimited supply of labor. This was expressed in the deceleration of urban population growth, whose annual average fell from roughly 5% to 3% when comparing the 1950s with the 1960s, and in the growth rate of the labor force, which fell from around 3% to less than 1% over the same comparison. The shrinking of the industrial reserve army reduced the degree of competition among workers, increased their bargaining power, and allowed real wages in industry and services to rise continuously, thereby expanding aggregate demand and, with it, labor demand. This circular dynamic was expressed in a systematic fall in unemployment from 1962 onward and in the upward spiral of a popular movement organized through political parties beyond the factory floor.[^33] The explosion of this empowerment process was triggered by the unionization of the countryside and agrarian reform under the Christian Democratic government, which produced an increase in union density and in average union size, indicators that rose sharply from roughly 10% to 25%[^34] and from 80 workers to 120, respectively. Popular empowerment during the long sixties was also expressed in the development of the pobladores movement and in the growing tendency toward land occupations beginning with the founding of La Victoria settlement on October 30, 1957---a phenomenon that slowly spread throughout Chile before exploding in the year of Popular Unity's electoral victory, when 220 land occupations were recorded nationwide, compared with 8 in 1968 and 35 in 1969.[^35]

The generational and educational dimension of the crisis of industrial patronage, in a context of growing radicalization of popular sectors, expanding electoral support for left parties culminating in government, the election of Salvador Allende as the first Marxist president chosen through liberal democracy, and a rising popular empowerment driven by a literate and politicized working class, is clearly illustrated by the case of the Yarur textile factory. There, young workers challenged the employer-dominated unionism that, through favors granted to older union leaders, constrained workers' bargaining power.[^36] The same applies to the People's Assembly that articulated industrial workers, peasants, pobladores, and militants of Popular Unity parties in Concepción, where the catalytic element in this process of political and territorial unity was the leadership of the Revolutionary Left Movement (MIR), whose militants came largely from the more educated sectors of the working class and petty bourgeoisie and were concentrated in the 20-to-35 age group.[^37] Another example is the emergence of Marxist dependency theory and its articulation in the Center for Socioeconomic Studies (CESO) at the University of Chile following the military coup in Brazil and the exile of Theotonio Dos Santos, Vania Bambirra, and Ruy Mauro Marini. They trained recently graduated economists such as Roberto Pizarro, Orlando Caputo, and Sergio Ramos, who would play a key role in developing and implementing the Popular Unity program,[^38] as well as technical cadres such as Fernando Flores and Gabriel Rodríguez, who---under the influence of dependency theory---developed the Cybersyn project for real-time economic planning under the idea of technological autonomy.[^39]

The Chilean socialist revolution was a historical process that occurred "from above" with the arrival of Popular Unity to government, and "from below" through the empowerment of the popular movement expressed in the mobilization of workers, peasants, and pobladores.[^40] The articulation of both processes took shape precisely in the implementation of the Popular Unity program in response to the challenge of resolving the crisis of industrial patronage and its collapse as an institutional logic capable of providing at least minimal functionality to market structures for the reproduction of accumulation and capitalist profit.[^41] On the diagnosis that the ruling class was incapable of advancing national interests because of its relations of dependence on foreign capital, the program that brought Salvador Allende to the presidency proposed that the working class was the historical subject capable of driving economic development, and that popular power was a precondition for a successful transition to socialism. The strategic plan aimed to promote national sovereignty on four fronts. First, it called for the creation of the Social Property Area (APS), composed of nationalized and public enterprises considered strategic for economic development. Second, it proposed a robust income-redistribution program in order to democratize access to consumer goods and social security for the vast majority of the population. Third, it sought to establish a state banking sector and a public foreign-trade enterprise to energize smaller industrial firms by providing access to finance and international markets. Fourth, it deepened agrarian reform to resolve productive stagnation in agriculture by redistributing land held in large estates.[^42]

The reaction of capitalist institutions to the Chilean revolution first took the form of a financial crisis triggered by Salvador Allende's victory in the 1970 presidential election. This was expressed in a 47% fall in Santiago stock market prices between September and October of that year, a fall that can be explained by a combination of spontaneous and deliberate behavior by the affluent classes, including massive withdrawals from savings accounts and a contraction in their demand for durable consumer goods such as automobiles.[^43] Yet this, paradoxically, reinforced the relevance of Popular Unity's economic reactivation program, which proved successful during its first year as economic growth was substantially reactivated through the expansion of aggregate demand via real wage adjustments and a strong increase in public expenditure,[^44] producing a 7.1% increase in per capita GDP in 1971,[^45] while the strengthened position of the working class was reflected in distributive conflict through a dramatic increase in labor's share of national income, from 42.71% in 1970 to 50.56% in 1971.[^46] The radical economic democratization promoted by Popular Unity also took shape in the labor process through an intensive increase in worker participation in decision-making within APS firms, allowing workers to influence decisions directly under a new co-managed labor-relations regime in which authority was jointly exercised by workers and state representatives, even if firm-level microeconomic interests still prevailed so long as they remained within the framework of national economic planning. Although this did not become extensive---since the APS represented less than 1% of establishments by mid-1973---it was intensive, because firms in the social area generated more than 40% of total industrial output and employed more than 30% of industrial labor. Contrary to what bourgeois mythology has claimed about the Chilean experience of economic democratization, the effects of worker participation in decision-making over the labor process were positive for productivity. Formal participation, however, was not by itself sufficient to generate productivity gains in APS firms, since political and ideological factors also played a decisive role: the evidence suggests that the influence of parties most deeply embedded in the state apparatus, such as the Christian Democrats and the Communist Party, undermined the redistribution of real power within the enterprise and therefore limited the effects of economic democracy on technical efficiency.[^47]

The reaction of the Chilean ruling class and U.S. imperialism to sabotage the Chilean revolution operated mainly through supply chains and the bottlenecks of Chilean economic development. Copper exports continued despite attempts at sabotage by large U.S. transnational corporations such as Kennecott and Anaconda, but it was the blockage of spare-parts imports that generated bottlenecks and, in a context of scarce dollar reserves for imports and low agricultural productivity that agrarian reform could not resolve rapidly because of its structural character, significantly affected national production, particularly in mining and manufacturing.[^48] Despite Soviet support in the form of spare parts delivered free of charge in 1972 and 1973, these could not always be put to use because of the technological incompatibility between Soviet parts and the U.S. machinery and equipment on which Chilean industrialization still depended, as well as the practical difficulty of reading instructions in Russian.[^49] Even more important was the 1972 employers' strike in the transport sector, which intensified problems that were already structural in a sector historically marked by low productivity under Chile's geographic conditions: because the country is connected by a single main highway, production can be brought to a halt through interruptions in the circulation of goods.[^50] Yet the deployment of the Cybersyn project as a mechanism for real-time planning of APS production made it possible to confront the employers' strike by coordinating supply chains,[^51] while the Supply and Price Boards (JAP) contained excessive price increases imposed by small commercial capitals aligned with the reaction across Chilean territories and also supported the supply of basic goods to the population through popular organization.[^52]

The inflationary context produced by hoarding, supply-chain bottlenecks generated by the reaction, and the fall in the price of copper amid the crisis of the Bretton Woods international financial system as an expression of the crisis of North Atlantic Fordism led to a critical shortage of dollar reserves and the collapse of the escudo. This created the conditions under which the reaction could articulate a counterrevolutionary social bloc composed of the professional classes, ultimately led by Pinochet and the military junta on September 11, 1973.[^53] What followed was not a destruction of the state prior to the implementation of orthodox neoliberal policies by the Chicago Boys; rather, the latter constituted themselves as a vanguard of the counterrevolution, operating from the very offices of ODEPLAN where the APS had been designed and implemented during Popular Unity,[^54] in order to destroy the industrial basis that had sustained popular power. The economy was liberalized under a rationality aimed above all at disciplining the working class, producing a premature deindustrialization of the Chilean economy.[^55] The restoration of ruling-class power therefore took the form not only of a civilizational regression marked by massive human rights violations directed especially at Marxist parties,[^56] but also of a transnationalization of state terror. Using the same telex technologies that had endowed Cybersyn with the capacity to sustain production during the 1972 employers' strike, the disciplining of the revolutionary upsurge through state terrorism---whose wider horizon had been opened by the Cuban Revolution---scaled up into a transnational network of terror across Latin America and throughout the Southern Cone under Operation Condor.[^57]

## Conclusion

The capitalist modernization project of Chile's ruling classes was permanently truncated by the contradictions and strategic incapacities of those same ruling classes, which proved wholly unable to resolve the bottlenecks that obstructed capitalist development. This article has argued that the historical anchor of this problem lies in the early integration of industrial capital with the landed elite as a ruling class, and especially in the continuities of the sanctioning system imposed on the working class within the sphere of social reproduction. This regime of social relations created its own gravediggers by driving a modernization in the quantity and quality of labor power that was incapable of fulfilling its promises of social inclusion through employment and development, thereby giving rise to a young and literate working class as the motor of a popular movement that sought to shape its own destiny by participating in the direction of production, taking its place in the city, and claiming the land for those who work it.

None of this could have happened in the way it did without the collapse of the ruling class's institutional logic and its patronage relations. Popular Unity can therefore be understood as a cultural revolution that emerged as a possible alternative to the crisis of Chilean capitalism and that confronted the task of deploying new institutional logics anchored in popular power. Those logics could not consolidate in a context defined not merely by sabotage from imperialism and the oligarchy, but by the global crisis of capitalism in its Fordist phase, a crisis that operated through the external constraints imposed by the green devil---dollar scarcity---as well as through Chile's own historical development bottlenecks, such as dependence on road trucking to sustain supply chains across the country.

Fifty years after the fall of Popular Unity, Chilean society remains at a crossroads. The current conjuncture presents clear continuities with the period discussed here: the patronal character of the institutions that govern us and their deep crisis anchored in economic dependence and, even more today, in cultural dependence as well. Yet the challenges facing left forces struggling to build a new kind of society arise from a balance of forces more akin to those that existed before the exhaustion of the unlimited labor supply during the long sixties than to those that followed it. In other words, popular empowerment under current conditions does not enjoy the same conditions of possibility so long as the industrial reserve army continues to impose itself as a disciplining mechanism on popular sectors. What is to be done under such conditions is not a question that this essay can or should answer. Still, the lesson offered here for reflection and debate is that the old maxim of historical materialism remains fully valid once one recognizes that the current crisis of capitalism in its neoliberal phase shows that social relations of production have again become fetters on the development of the productive forces. In the author's view, political militancy and organization remain the spaces from which the cultural revolution can be triggered, making it possible to build new social relations, break the chains that bind the development of the productive forces, and unleash human flourishing.
