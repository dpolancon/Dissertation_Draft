## Why this paper matters

This paper is highly reusable because it does not merely summarize Marx on the state. It reconstructs a missing problem-space: what Marx's unwritten book on the state would likely have needed to address, and how later Marxist debates tried to fill that gap. That makes the paper useful not only as a source on Marxist state theory, but as a routing device for later work on political form, capitalist domination, hegemony, world-market integration, state finance, and the strategic selectivity of state institutions.

For dissertation use, the paper is especially valuable where one needs a compact bridge between Marx, Gramsci, Poulantzas, and strategic-relational approaches, while preserving the distinction between the form of the capitalist state and the historically specific exercise of state power.

## One-paragraph overview

Jessop begins from a well-known absence: Marx never completed the promised book on the state, even though such a book was part of the broader plan surrounding *Capital*. Rather than speculate idly about the missing volume, Jessop reconstructs the main thematic lines that such a work would likely have contained. The article argues that Marx's most durable insight is not a crude instrumentalist claim that the state is simply a tool of the ruling class, nor an abstract autonomist claim that the state floats above society, but a more relational and form-sensitive approach. On this reading, the capitalist state has a historically specific political form that is formally adequate to capitalist social relations, even while actual state power remains conjunctural, contested, and strategically selective. Jessop develops this through Marx's writings on France, Bonapartism, the world market and the plurality of states, the tax state, ideological domination, and later Marxist extensions in Gramsci, Althusser, and Poulantzas.

## Central problem

Jessop's opening problem is that Marx's critique of capitalism remained systematically incomplete at the level of the state. Marx had planned a book on the state after the books on capital, wage labour, and landed property, but never wrote it. The result is a theoretical deficit: Marx's published critique of political economy largely abstracts from the specific form of the capitalist state, from the plurality of states, and from the political processes through which class power is organized, contested, and mediated.

Jessop therefore treats the article as a guided reconstruction. The task is not to invent a missing doctrine for Marx, but to organize the dispersed materials that Marx actually left behind into a coherent set of themes.

## Core thesis

The paper's strongest thesis is that Marx's most fruitful contribution to state theory lies in a historically specific analysis of political form. The capitalist state should be understood neither as a mere instrument nor as a self-standing autonomous force, but as a historically specific social relation whose institutional form is formally adequate to capitalist relations of production and exchange, while the exercise of state power remains mediated by class struggle, conjunctures, and the strategic organization of hegemony.

## Argument map

### 1. Marx's missing book on the state

Jessop opens by stressing that a book on the state was part of Marx's six-book plan and that its absence matters. The deficit is not only textual but theoretical: it leaves underdeveloped the relation between capitalism and the political form of the state, the plurality of states, and the strategic analysis of conjunctures.

He then identifies seven broad kinds of political analysis that Marx *did* undertake, including critiques of political theory, historical studies of state forms, conjunctural analyses, interstate relations, and general analyses of the capitalist type of state. The article uses these dispersed materials to sketch what the missing state book might have contained.

### 2. The state as a social relation

The pivot of the paper comes when Jessop extends Marx's famous claim that capital is not a thing but a social relation. By analogy, the state is best grasped not as a thing but as a social relation mediated through institutions and juridico-political powers.

This move is crucial because it shifts the analysis away from reified images of the state and toward structured social relations. The capitalist state is not simply a neutral container nor a puppet. Its specificity lies in the way class domination is politically organized through impersonal public authority.

### 3. Formal adequacy

Jessop emphasizes that Marx's most enduring line is a form-analysis of the capitalist state. A political order based on the rule of law, equal citizenship, representative institutions, and an impersonal sovereign state is formally adequate to an economic order based on commodity exchange, private property, and wage labour.

This does **not** mean that capitalism automatically produces a stable liberal democracy. It means that this political form fits capitalism in a specific sense: exploitation can take the form of exchange, and class domination can appear under juridical equality and democratic representation.

### 4. The contradiction of democratic capitalism

A core tension appears here. Democratic constitutions grant political rights broadly, including to subordinate classes, but only on condition that those rights are not used to abolish the economic relations on which capitalist domination rests. The dominant classes, conversely, can enjoy constitutional guarantees of property so long as they do not overtly demand the restoration of openly exclusive political rule.

This tension helps explain why liberal-democratic forms can persist, why they can enter crisis, and why exceptional regimes may emerge when hegemony fails.

### 5. France, Bonapartism, and conjunctural analysis

Jessop treats Marx's writings on France as the richest site for understanding the specificity of political struggles on the terrain of the modern state. Here Marx does not read political actors directly off economic classes. Instead, he studies the mediated relation between class forces and political representation.

Bonapartism becomes the limit case of relative state autonomy: not because the state floats free of class relations, but because a conjunctural stalemate among class forces allows a centralized executive power to represent itself as standing above society while still reproducing capitalist domination in altered form.

### 6. World market and world of states

One of the paper's most reusable distinctions is between the **world market** and the **world of states**. For Jessop, following Marx, capitalism tends toward world-market integration, but there is no corresponding world state. Instead, the world political order remains a plural and uneven field of states.

This means capitalism develops through the interaction of two distinct but interrelated logics: the logic of capital accumulation on a world scale, and the geopolitical logic of a plurality of rival, unequal, and strategically differentiated states.

### 7. Specific determinations of the capitalist state

Jessop then isolates several determinations of the capitalist type of state that are especially important for theory-building:

#### 7.1 The modern tax state
The capitalist state is a tax state. Its existence is expressed economically through taxation, debt, and fiscal organization. This makes state finance a privileged site for reading the relation between political authority and capitalist reproduction.

#### 7.2 Nation and nation-state
National political forms matter because they provide a basis for citizenship, representation, and the illusory general interest. But they also divide the working class and mediate political struggle in historically specific ways.

#### 7.3 The ideal collective capitalist
The state can act to secure the general conditions of capitalist reproduction even when many individual capitals resist such intervention. The factory laws are the classic case: the state may regulate capital in the long-run interest of capital.

#### 7.4 Ideological domination
State power also operates ideologically. The state and related apparatuses help produce legitimacy, organize consent, and naturalize domination.

### 8. Later Marxist developments

Jessop closes by situating later Marxist state theory as attempts to think beyond Marx's missing book.

- **Gramsci** deepens the analysis through hegemony, civil society, and coercion.
- **Althusser** distinguishes repressive and ideological apparatuses.
- **Poulantzas** advances a relational theory of the state and a richer account of hegemony and exceptional regimes.
- **The Poulantzas–Miliband debate** is presented not as a simple structuralism-versus-instrumentalism showdown, but as an overdrawn opposition that obscures the complexity of both positions.

## Key concepts

### Missing book on the state
**Definition:** the absent volume in Marx's projected architecture that would have treated the state more systematically.

**Why it matters:** it explains why Marxist state theory is necessarily reconstructive and why later debates proliferated.

**Use in your project:** a good entry point whenever you want to justify treating Marx on the state as a dispersed field rather than a closed doctrine.

### State as social relation
**Definition:** the state is not a thing but a structured social relation among class forces, mediated by institutions and juridico-political forms.

**Why it matters:** this blocks both reification and voluntarism. The state is neither neutral machinery nor a simple puppet.

**Use in your project:** highly compatible with a relational ontology and with institutional analysis that avoids dualistic economy-versus-politics formulations.

### Formal adequacy
**Definition:** the historically specific fit between capitalist economic relations and representative, juridically universal, impersonal state forms.

**Why it matters:** it explains why democracy and capitalism can be compatible in form without implying substantive equality.

**Use in your project:** useful whenever you need to explain how a political form can stabilize accumulation without reducing politics to a mere reflection of the economy.

### Illusory general interest
**Definition:** the state's claim to represent society as a whole while materially reproducing a divided class order.

**Why it matters:** this is the ideological and political hinge that links legal universality to class domination.

**Use in your project:** valuable for discussing macroeconomic indicators, policy routines, or state practices that appear universal while carrying class-structured effects.

### Bonapartism / exceptional regime
**Definition:** forms of rule that emerge when hegemonic management fails and democratic mediation is suspended, narrowed, or reworked.

**Why it matters:** Jessop uses Bonapartism to show that relative state autonomy is a conjunctural phenomenon rooted in class stalemate, not proof that the state stands above class relations.

**Use in your project:** relevant for thinking about crisis management, authoritarian turns, and emergency state forms.

### World market / world of states
**Definition:** two distinct but interacting logics: integrated capitalist accumulation on the one hand, and a plural, unequal field of states on the other.

**Why it matters:** this blocks the fantasy of a frictionless global capitalism and keeps geopolitical mediation in view.

**Use in your project:** particularly useful where global accumulation, monetary hierarchy, trade, or state competition are analytically central.

### Tax state
**Definition:** the capitalist state's economic existence is expressed through taxation, debt, and fiscal powers grounded in public authority.

**Why it matters:** it clarifies why state finance is not secondary but constitutive of modern statehood.

**Use in your project:** directly relevant if fiscal techniques, budgets, statistical routines, or public debt enter the analysis.

### Ideal collective capitalist
**Definition:** the state's role in securing the general conditions of capitalist reproduction, even against the preferences of some individual capitals.

**Why it matters:** it explains why state regulation may stabilize capitalism rather than negate it.

**Use in your project:** useful when treating institutions as active organizers of accumulation regimes rather than passive reflections.

### Hegemony protected by coercion
**Definition:** Gramsci's formula, used by Jessop to describe state power as the combination of consent and coercion.

**Why it matters:** it updates the Marxian problematic by centering national-popular leadership and ideological organization.

**Use in your project:** important for linking institutional stabilization to ideological and political work, not only to coercive enforcement.

## Reusable claims

1. Marx's unwritten book on the state is not a minor archival curiosity; it marks a real theoretical gap in the critique of capitalism.
2. Marx's most durable contribution to state theory lies in form-analysis, not in a crude doctrine of direct ruling-class manipulation.
3. The capitalist state is best understood as a social relation mediated through institutions, not as a thing.
4. Liberal-democratic state forms are formally adequate to capitalism because they universalize juridical equality while rendering substantive class domination opaque.
5. World-market integration does not eliminate the plurality of states; it intensifies the interaction between accumulation and geopolitical rivalry.
6. Fiscal organization, taxation, debt, and the tax state are constitutive of capitalist statehood, not merely administrative details.
7. The state may regulate capital in the general interest of capitalist reproduction even against many individual capitals.
8. Later Marxist state theory matters most where it extends Marx's relational, conjunctural, and hegemonic insights rather than flattening them into slogans.

## Relevance for dissertation use

### Direct uses

- To justify treating the state as a historically specific social relation rather than a separate ontological sphere.
- To bridge Marx, Gramsci, and Poulantzas without losing the specificity of political form.
- To frame the state as constitutive of accumulation, not a mere external container.
- To connect ideological and fiscal routines to broader forms of capitalist stabilization.

### Especially useful for Chapter 2-style work

This paper is particularly helpful where the argument concerns the institutional and ideological embeddedness of operational routines. Jessop gives a compact vocabulary for showing how apparently universal state practices can help organize capitalist reproduction while presenting themselves as neutral or general.

### What not to over-import

- Do not treat the paper as a finished Marxist theory of the state. Jessop himself presents it as a sketch and reconstruction.
- Do not collapse the paper into a Poulantzas-only reading; Jessop is reconstructing a broader terrain.
- Do not use "formal adequacy" as if it meant functional necessity or automatic stability. The paper consistently leaves room for crisis, disjunction, and exceptional forms.

## Open questions and tensions

1. How far can the concept of formal adequacy travel historically before it becomes too general?
2. Does the paper underplay the material specificity of different state apparatuses in favor of high-level conceptual reconstruction?
3. How exactly should one connect the tax-state argument to contemporary monetary and financial governance?
4. What happens to the argument once post-Fordist governance and transnationalized policy routines become central?
5. How should Jessop's reconstruction be integrated with colonial and racialized state formation beyond the examples briefly mentioned?
