# Shaikh (1978) — Crisis Theories Notebook

## Source
**Anwar Shaikh, _An Introduction to the History of Crisis Theories_ (1978)**

---

## 1. Overview

Anwar Shaikh’s paper is not just a survey of past crisis theories. It is a **critical history** of how economists and Marxists have explained capitalist breakdown, and it uses that history to defend a specific position: capitalism is neither a self-stabilizing system nor a system blocked mainly by an external shortage of demand. It is a **self-expanding but self-limiting** system whose crises arise from contradictions internal to accumulation itself.

Shaikh begins by tying **reproduction** and **crisis** together. Any theory that explains how capitalism reproduces itself also implies a theory of how it fails. On that basis he organizes the history of crisis theory into three broad families:
1. theories that see capitalism as automatically self-reproducing,
2. theories that see it as unable to expand without an external source of demand,
3. theories that see accumulation as internally self-undermining.

The first family includes **laissez-faire orthodoxy** and **Keynesianism**. Laissez-faire theory treats crises as either external shocks or temporary fluctuations around equilibrium. Keynes breaks with the idea that capitalism automatically reaches full employment, but still treats the system as manageable: state policy can stabilize demand and keep capitalism going. Shaikh reads both as theories that ultimately deny any necessary internal historical limit to capitalism.

The second family is **underconsumption theory**. Shaikh reconstructs its basic logic clearly: workers cannot buy back the full product, so a “demand gap” appears; if capitalists save and invest too much, productive capacity expands faster than consumer demand, and crisis follows. His main criticism is that the logic of this argument actually implies not merely that capitalism can overaccumulate, but that it cannot sustain self-expansion at all. Since actual capitalism plainly does grow, underconsumption theories repeatedly appeal to **external factors** such as foreign markets, imperialism, war, innovation, or state demand management.

He then shows how **Marx’s reproduction schemes** shattered the old underconsumption argument. Marx demonstrates that capitalism can generate its own internal markets because production is organized for profit, not for final consumption, and because Department I can produce means of production for itself as well as for Department II. That move, for Shaikh, defeats both simple underconsumptionism and the related claim that disproportionality alone explains crisis.

Shaikh’s positive position comes in the section on **capitalism as self-limiting accumulation**. Here he argues that Marx’s key insight is the **tendency of the rate of profit to fall**. Capital accumulation raises productivity and often raises the rate of exploitation too, but it also tends to increase the mass of machinery and materials relative to living labor. Since living labor is the source of surplus value, profitability comes under pressure. Capitalism undermines itself not because demand is inherently insufficient, but because accumulation itself progressively burdens profitability.

A major part of the paper is devoted to defending this position against rivals. Shaikh reviews objections to the falling-rate-of-profit theory, including arguments about technique choice, capital measurement, and the interpretation of official statistics. He grants that important debates remain, but insists that the broad thrust of Marx’s law is still stronger than its alternatives.

He also criticizes the **profit-squeeze** school, which explains falling profitability through rising wages and stronger labor. Shaikh argues that this confuses Marxian categories with bourgeois accounting categories, especially by treating the observable profit-wage ratio as if it were identical to the rate of exploitation. In his view, that mistake turns an effect into a cause.

The paper ends by drawing broader lessons. Crisis theories have political implications, but no automatic one-to-one political meaning. Statistics are theory-laden, not innocent facts. And understanding a present crisis requires understanding the history of crisis explanations so that one does not simply repeat earlier errors in new language.

## 2. Reverse outline

### 1. Introduction: what counts as a crisis?
- **Main job:** Define crisis as a generalized breakdown in the economic and political relations of capitalist reproduction.
- **Main claim:** To explain crisis, one must explain how capitalism reproduces itself in the first place.
- **Why it matters:** Sets up the paper as a history of theories of reproduction and non-reproduction.

### 2. Three major traditions in crisis theory
- **Main job:** Lay out the paper’s organizing map.
- **Main claim:** Crisis theories fall into three broad groups: automatic self-reproduction, external-limit theories, and internal-limit theories.
- **Why it matters:** Gives the reader the structure for the whole essay.

### 3. Laissez-faire orthodoxy
- **Main job:** Describe the classical and neoclassical claim that capitalism is self-regulating.
- **Main claim:** Crises are either external shocks or minor cyclical deviations, not expressions of capitalism’s inner laws.
- **Why it matters:** Establishes the main bourgeois position Shaikh wants to reject.

### 4. Keynesianism
- **Main job:** Show how Keynes modifies orthodoxy without abandoning its basic horizon.
- **Main claim:** Capitalism is unstable because investment is volatile, but state policy can manage demand and preserve the system.
- **Why it matters:** Marks Keynes as a break with laissez-faire, but not with the premise that capitalism can be sustained indefinitely.

### 5. Capitalism as incapable of self-expansion
- **Main job:** Introduce the underconsumption tradition.
- **Main claim:** Capitalism needs an external source of effective demand because its own internal dynamics do not generate enough demand to support growth.
- **Why it matters:** Opens the critique of a major anti-orthodox tradition.

### 6. The concept of the demand gap
- **Main job:** Reconstruct the logic of underconsumption.
- **Main claim:** Workers cannot buy back the full product, and capitalist saving/investment opens a gap between output and consumer demand.
- **Why it matters:** Shows the intuitive appeal of underconsumption theory before Shaikh turns against it.

### 7. The dilemma of early underconsumptionists
- **Main job:** Expose the internal problem in the theory.
- **Main claim:** If their logic is taken seriously, the sustainable rate of capitalist accumulation is not merely limited but effectively zero.
- **Why it matters:** This is Shaikh’s core objection to classical underconsumptionism.

### 8. Malthus and Sismondi
- **Main job:** Historicize conservative and radical versions of underconsumption.
- **Main claim:** Malthus uses underconsumption to defend landlords; Sismondi uses it to criticize capitalist inequality and instability.
- **Why it matters:** Shows that similar economic theories can support very different politics.

### 9. Hobson, monopoly, and imperialism
- **Main job:** Trace underconsumption into the age of imperialism.
- **Main claim:** Monopoly enlarges surplus and narrows markets, pushing capitalism toward imperialism as an outlet.
- **Why it matters:** Connects crisis theory to later debates about monopoly capital.

### 10. Marx against underconsumption
- **Main job:** Show why Marx breaks decisively with the old theory.
- **Main claim:** Expanded reproduction is possible because production for profit can generate internal markets.
- **Why it matters:** This is the turning point of the paper’s argument.

### 11. Russian Marxism and the attack on populism
- **Main job:** Recount the debate over whether capitalism was viable in Russia.
- **Main claim:** Lenin, Struve, Bulgakov, and Tugan-Baranowsky defeat the populist claim that capitalism requires foreign markets to survive.
- **Why it matters:** Shows the political stakes of reproduction theory.

### 12. Disproportionality theory
- **Main job:** Present the next candidate explanation once underconsumption is weakened.
- **Main claim:** Crises arise because capitalist anarchy produces sectoral disproportions, not because demand is inherently insufficient.
- **Why it matters:** Advances the debate, but also sets up another position Shaikh finds inadequate.

### 13. Luxemburg’s revival of underconsumption
- **Main job:** Explain Rosa Luxemburg’s challenge to Marx’s reproduction schemes.
- **Main claim:** Expanded reproduction is formally possible but socially impossible unless capitalism sells to non-capitalist strata.
- **Why it matters:** Reopens the collapse debate on explicitly revolutionary grounds.

### 14. Shaikh’s critique of Luxemburg
- **Main job:** Reject Luxemburg’s solution.
- **Main claim:** Capitalists invest for profit, not because final consumption already exists, so accumulation does not need an external buyer in the way Luxemburg claims.
- **Why it matters:** Clears the ground for an internal-limit theory.

### 15. Sweezy, Baran, and monopoly capital
- **Main job:** Examine modern Marxian underconsumptionism.
- **Main claim:** These theories identify stagnation and excess capacity, but they still depend on an unresolved claim that capitalism persistently overexpands capacity relative to demand.
- **Why it matters:** Shaikh argues they lack a satisfactory theory of the actual rate of accumulation.

### 16. Transition to self-limiting accumulation
- **Main job:** Shift from demand-centered theories to profitability-centered ones.
- **Main claim:** Capitalism does not fail because it cannot expand, but because its expansion undermines its own basis.
- **Why it matters:** Announces Shaikh’s preferred framework.

### 17. Marx’s theory of the falling rate of profit
- **Main job:** Present the paper’s positive core.
- **Main claim:** Accumulation raises productivity and exploitation, yet tends to raise the value composition of capital and lower profitability.
- **Why it matters:** Supplies the internal mechanism of crisis.

### 18. Objections to the falling-rate-of-profit theory
- **Main job:** Engage major criticisms.
- **Main claim:** Problems remain debated, but objections based on technique choice, official capital-output ratios, or simplified logic do not dislodge the theory.
- **Why it matters:** Defends the law as still the strongest account of structural crisis.

### 19. The history of falling-rate-of-profit theory
- **Main job:** Place Marx in relation to Smith and Ricardo.
- **Main claim:** Classical economists saw falling profitability, but Marx uniquely explains it through rising productivity rather than declining productivity or rising wages.
- **Why it matters:** Positions Marx’s theory as a genuine breakthrough.

### 20. Grossmann, Mattick, and Yaffe
- **Main job:** Show how later Marxists revive the falling-rate-of-profit approach.
- **Main claim:** Crises emerge when declining profitability halts the growth of profits and curtails investment; crisis phenomena like inflation, unemployment, and state spending are effects, not causes.
- **Why it matters:** Connects Marx’s law to twentieth-century crisis theory and politics.

### 21. Profit-squeeze theory
- **Main job:** Present and criticize the rival view that labor militancy causes crisis.
- **Main claim:** Profit-squeeze theory wrongly identifies the profit-wage ratio with the rate of exploitation and misreads falling money profits as evidence that wages caused the crisis.
- **Why it matters:** Shaikh rejects the idea that workers’ strength is the fundamental cause of declining profitability.

### 22. Conclusions and lessons
- **Main job:** Draw out what the history of crisis theory teaches.
- **Main claim:** Crisis theory is politically consequential, facts are theory-laden, and studying present crises requires studying prior explanations of crisis.
- **Why it matters:** Ends by turning the paper into a methodological warning as well as a substantive argument.

## 3. Why Shaikh finds disproportionality theory inadequate

Shaikh thinks **disproportionality theory is too weak** because it explains crisis mainly as a problem of **mis-coordination** between sectors, not as a necessary outcome of the deep logic of accumulation. In that theory, capitalism can in principle reproduce itself indefinitely **if** Departments I and II grow in the right proportions; crisis happens because anarchic competition makes that proportionality accidental. For Shaikh, that turns crisis into a problem of bad alignment rather than an inner limit of capital itself.

That is why he presents Tugan-Baranowsky and Hilferding as saying, in effect: capitalism has no built-in barrier except anarchy, so **planning** could remove crisis. Hilferding’s idea of “organized capitalism” is the clearest form of this. Shaikh sees that as inadequate because it implies that crisis is not really necessary to capitalism as such, only to its unplanned form. Once that is accepted, the question “what is there to prevent capitalism from growing forever?” remains unanswered.

A second problem is that disproportionality theory does not identify a **driving mechanism** that makes the disproportions recur in a systematic way. Sectoral imbalances obviously happen, but Shaikh wants more than the claim that capitalist production is chaotic and may get out of line. He wants a theory of why the system **tends toward breakdown**. Mere imbalance is too contingent: it can describe the form a crisis takes without explaining the underlying tendency that produces crisis again and again.

A third point is that Marx’s reproduction schemes show **balanced growth is possible in principle**. Shaikh uses that result to defeat old underconsumption arguments, but it also limits disproportionality theory. If balanced reproduction is logically possible, then disproportionality by itself cannot be the deepest explanation of crisis. It shows one way reproduction can be interrupted, but not why capitalism is structurally crisis-prone.

So Shaikh moves to a stronger claim: the real limit is not mis-proportion between sectors but **profitability**. In his reading of Marx, capital is driven to accumulate, but accumulation itself tends to reduce profitability over time. That gives him an internal, law-like explanation of crisis. Disproportions may appear during crises, but they are not the root cause.

There is also a political reason he finds disproportionality theory unsatisfying. If crises come only from anarchy, then better organization, planning, or state coordination seems enough to solve them. Shaikh thinks that leads too easily to reformist conclusions. By contrast, a falling-profitability account says crisis is rooted in capitalist social relations themselves, so it cannot be permanently fixed by better administration alone.

**In one sentence:** disproportionality theory describes how crises can appear, but not why capitalism necessarily generates them.

---

## 4. Compare and contrast: disproportionality theory vs. falling-rate-of-profit theory

Disproportionality theory and falling-rate-of-profit theory are both attempts to explain crisis **after** simple underconsumptionism is weakened, but they differ sharply on what kind of problem capitalism is. **Disproportionality theory** says capitalism is capable of reproduction in principle, yet its anarchic lack of coordination causes mismatches between sectors, which periodically interrupt accumulation. Its weakness, for Shaikh, is that the contradiction is largely one of **organization**: if capitalist production could somehow be proportioned correctly, crisis would not be necessary. **Falling-rate-of-profit theory**, by contrast, says the contradiction lies in accumulation itself. Capital is driven to raise productivity through mechanization and accumulation, but in doing so it tends to increase the weight of constant capital relative to living labor, the source of surplus value, and so it undermines profitability. On this view, disproportions can still happen, but they are secondary expressions of a deeper process. The difference is decisive: disproportionality theory makes crisis look contingent and potentially solvable by planning, while falling-rate-of-profit theory makes crisis a structural outcome of capitalist social relations.

---

## 5. Condensed study takeaway

Shaikh’s overall argument can be reduced to a sequence:

- Orthodoxy says capitalism reproduces itself and crises are secondary.
- Underconsumption says capitalism cannot grow on its own and needs outside demand.
- Marx’s reproduction schemes show capitalism **can** generate internal markets.
- Disproportionality theory then says crisis comes from anarchy and imbalance.
- Shaikh says that still is not deep enough.
- The strongest theory is that capitalism is **self-expanding but self-limiting** because accumulation itself tends to undermine profitability.

---

## 6. One-paragraph thesis statement

Shaikh’s essay argues that the history of crisis theory moves from apologetic accounts of self-equilibrating capitalism, through underconsumptionist accounts of inadequate demand, toward a properly Marxian theory of internal limits. He rejects both orthodox equilibrium theory and demand-gap theories because they fail to grasp that capitalism can generate its own markets while still remaining structurally crisis-prone. He also finds disproportionality theory insufficient because it makes crisis a matter of mis-coordination rather than a necessary expression of capital’s inner laws. The strongest explanation, for Shaikh, is the tendency of accumulation itself to undermine profitability, making capitalist development both expansive and self-limiting.
