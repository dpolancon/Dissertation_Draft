## Source
**Makoto Itoh, _The Formation of Marx’s Theory of Crisis_ (1978)**

## 1. Overview

Makoto Itoh’s **“The Formation of Marx’s Theory of Crisis”** is both a reconstruction and a correction. He argues that Marx’s crisis theory in *Capital* is powerful but unfinished, and that it contains **two different and ultimately incompatible theories of crisis**: an **excess commodity theory** and an **excess capital theory**. Itoh’s project is to trace how both emerged across the *Grundrisse*, *Theories of Surplus Value*, and *Capital*, and to show why the second is the stronger path for completing Marx’s theory.

The paper begins with a methodological point about **levels of abstraction**. Itoh says Marx’s crisis theory in *Capital* is aimed at proving the logical necessity of **cyclical crisis at the level of basic principle**, using the mid-nineteenth-century industrial cycle as its empirical basis. Broader historical studies of crises across mercantilism, liberalism, and imperialism belong to a different level, what Uno calls **stages theory**, not to the pure theory of capitalist principles.

Itoh then identifies the two crisis theories inside Marx. The **excess commodity theory** explains crisis through overproduction relative to demand, whether because of underconsumption or disproportionality among branches. The **excess capital theory** explains crisis through **absolute overproduction of capital in relation to the laboring population**, which produces a sharp fall in profit and turns into crisis through the credit system. Itoh insists these are not just two emphases; they are logically opposed accounts of what is fundamental.

In Itoh’s reading, the *Grundrisse* leans heavily toward the **excess commodity** side. Marx is still close to Sismondi and Malthus in emphasizing the restriction of final consumption and the difficulty of realizing surplus value. At this stage Marx has not yet established the cyclical necessity of crisis and tends to slide toward a view where crisis almost means breakdown or collapse. At the same time, however, he begins to criticize simple underconsumptionism and to hint that the deeper issue is not just demand shortfall but the relation between production and capital’s self-expansion.

In *Theories of Surplus Value*, Itoh sees further development but still no decisive solution. Marx shifts from simple underconsumption toward a **disproportionality** account, arguing that overproduction in leading sectors can spread through the economy. Yet Itoh thinks this remains inadequate, because ordinary capitalist competition and credit usually adjust disproportions. Serious, cyclical crisis still seems to require some deeper internal obstacle in accumulation as a whole.

The decisive advance comes only in *Capital*, where Marx develops the **excess capital theory** more clearly. Itoh says this depends on two theoretical gains: first, a fuller theory of the **capitalist law of population**, including cyclical absorption and reproduction of a reserve army of labor; second, a theory of the **credit system**, which shows how tensions in accumulation are transmitted and intensified through money markets, bills, bank credit, and speculative stockpiling. These two pieces let Marx explain how prosperity itself produces conditions that culminate in crisis.

Itoh’s preferred explanation runs like this: during prosperity, accumulation expands with existing fixed capital and rising demand for labor. As labor markets tighten, **wages rise**, which squeezes the profit rate. At the same time, demand for money capital rises, and so can **interest rates**. This contradiction between a falling general profit rate and rising interest costs becomes explosive once speculative stockpiling and wholesale credit are added. Crisis begins not in retail trade but in wholesale trade and banking, where speculative positions collapse, prices fall, credit freezes, and reproduction is disrupted across the system.

A striking feature of Itoh’s argument is that he does **not** think the falling rate of profit by itself proves cyclical crisis. He explicitly says Marx’s law of the tendency of the rate of profit to fall is theoretically valid, but it is too long-term and abstract to establish the **logical inevitability and cyclical character** of crises on its own. Crisis has to be explained more concretely through overaccumulation relative to labor, fixed-capital constraints, and the credit mechanism.

Itoh also stresses the role of **depression and renewal**. Crisis does not end capitalism; it clears the ground for a new cycle. Depression devalues capital, leaves labor unemployed, and eventually pushes firms to renew fixed capital and adopt new methods of production. Because fixed capital wears out and is renewed in clusters, its “life-cycle” provides a material basis for periodic crisis and for the timing of renewed prosperity. Business cycles thus become the concrete mechanism through which the law of value regulates both class relations and relations among branches of production.

The paper’s final claim is that Marx’s crisis theory should be completed by **developing the excess-capital theory together with the credit theory**, while discarding the excess-commodity approach as a residue of anti-classical crisis theory. For Itoh, crises are not fundamentally caused by circulation failures or consumer demand shortfalls. They arise because capital’s own drive to accumulate runs up against the difficulty of treating labor-power as a commodity and is violently mediated through competition, fixed capital, and credit.

## 2. Reverse outline

### 1. Why crisis theory matters
- **Main job:** Establish crisis theory as central to Marx’s critique of classical political economy.
- **Main claim:** Without a systematic crisis theory, one cannot explain the logical necessity of cyclical crisis in capitalism.
- **Why it matters:** Frames the whole essay as a reconstruction of Marx’s unfinished theory.

### 2. Levels of abstraction
- **Main job:** Clarify how crisis should be studied.
- **Main claim:** The crisis theory in *Capital* belongs to the level of principle, while broader historical crisis analysis belongs to stages theory or concrete analysis.
- **Why it matters:** Protects the paper from being judged by standards of full historical narrative.

### 3. Two types of crisis theory in *Capital*
- **Main job:** Introduce the paper’s core distinction.
- **Main claim:** Marx’s text contains both an excess capital theory and an excess commodity theory.
- **Why it matters:** This distinction organizes the rest of Itoh’s argument.

### 4. The excess commodity theory
- **Main job:** Define one pole of Marx’s ambiguity.
- **Main claim:** Crisis can appear as overproduction beyond demand, whether through disproportionality or restricted mass consumption.
- **Why it matters:** Shows the persistence of anti-classical, realization-centered arguments inside Marx.

### 5. The excess capital theory
- **Main job:** Define the rival pole.
- **Main claim:** Crisis stems from overproduction of capital relative to the laboring population, producing a sharp fall in profit and a crisis process.
- **Why it matters:** Prepares Itoh’s case for which theory should be retained.

### 6. Crisis theory in the *Grundrisse*
- **Main job:** Trace Marx’s earliest systematic approach.
- **Main claim:** The *Grundrisse* still leans toward an underconsumptionist, excess-commodity view influenced by Sismondi and Malthus.
- **Why it matters:** Shows Marx beginning from a theory Itoh thinks he later partially outgrows.

### 7. Critique of simple underconsumption
- **Main job:** Mark tensions already present in the *Grundrisse*.
- **Main claim:** Marx criticizes the crude claim that crisis follows just because workers cannot buy back their product.
- **Why it matters:** Indicates movement toward a deeper theory beyond demand shortfall.

### 8. Profit theory in the *Grundrisse*
- **Main job:** Introduce the link between crisis and profitability.
- **Main claim:** Marx develops the falling tendency of the profit rate, but this is not yet enough to explain cyclical crisis.
- **Why it matters:** Foreshadows Itoh’s later insistence that falling profit alone is insufficient.

### 9. *Theories of Surplus Value*: progress and limits
- **Main job:** Show the next step in Marx’s development.
- **Main claim:** Marx still emphasizes realization and circulation, but increasingly treats circulation as part of reproduction.
- **Why it matters:** Moves crisis theory closer to the reproduction process without yet resolving its contradictions.

### 10. The disproportionality turn
- **Main job:** Explain Marx’s shift away from simple underconsumption.
- **Main claim:** Overproduction in leading commodities can spread through the interdependence of sectors and generate general crisis.
- **Why it matters:** Expands excess-commodity theory into a more sophisticated form.

### 11. Why disproportionality is not enough
- **Main job:** State the remaining problem.
- **Main claim:** Since competition and credit usually adjust disproportions, disproportionality alone cannot prove the necessity and cyclical form of crisis.
- **Why it matters:** Creates the need for the excess-capital theory.

### 12. The unanswered question of overproduction of capital
- **Main job:** Focus the theoretical issue.
- **Main claim:** Marx comes to ask what “overabundance of capital” really means and how it differs from overproduction of commodities.
- **Why it matters:** This becomes the hinge of Itoh’s reconstruction.

### 13. What was missing before *Capital*
- **Main job:** Explain why Marx could not yet answer that question.
- **Main claim:** He still lacked a developed credit theory and a sufficiently cyclical theory of the capitalist law of population.
- **Why it matters:** Identifies the theoretical preconditions of the mature crisis theory.

### 14. Completion in *Capital*
- **Main job:** Present the decisive theoretical advance.
- **Main claim:** The excess-capital theory becomes possible once Marx integrates overaccumulation, reserve army dynamics, and credit.
- **Why it matters:** Marks the point where Itoh thinks Marx produces something genuinely new.

### 15. Why excess commodity theory should be rejected
- **Main job:** Judge the two competing lines.
- **Main claim:** The excess-commodity theory mistakes intermediary factors or results for causes and remains trapped in circulation.
- **Why it matters:** Itoh explicitly sides with excess capital theory.

### 16. The role of the reserve army and wages
- **Main job:** Explain the internal build-up of crisis during prosperity.
- **Main claim:** Accumulation tightens labor markets, wages rise, and profit rates are squeezed.
- **Why it matters:** Connects prosperity itself to the onset of overaccumulation.

### 17. Fixed capital and cyclical timing
- **Main job:** Give the cycle a material rhythm.
- **Main claim:** Because firms normally accumulate on the basis of existing fixed capital, and renew it in clusters, fixed capital helps structure the periodicity of crises.
- **Why it matters:** Supplies a temporal basis for business cycles.

### 18. Credit as an internal mechanism
- **Main job:** Show how contradictions become acute.
- **Main claim:** Credit is not an external add-on but an internal mechanism that mobilizes idle capital and synchronizes reproduction.
- **Why it matters:** Makes credit theory indispensable to crisis theory.

### 19. From prosperity to crisis
- **Main job:** Reconstruct the turning point of the cycle.
- **Main claim:** Rising wages, falling profits, rising interest, and speculative stockpiling produce a tightening of credit and a collapse in wholesale trade and banking.
- **Why it matters:** This is Itoh’s concrete mechanism of crisis outbreak.

### 20. Crisis and depression
- **Main job:** Explain what follows the crash.
- **Main claim:** Crisis destroys value and employment, then depression persists because distorted fixed-capital structures cannot be quickly realigned.
- **Why it matters:** Prevents crisis from being treated as an instantaneous reset.

### 21. Renewal and recovery
- **Main job:** Explain how a new expansion becomes possible.
- **Main claim:** Recovery comes through depreciation and renewal of fixed capital, adoption of new methods, and readjustment of proportionality across branches.
- **Why it matters:** Turns crisis into a necessary moment of capitalist development, not just a rupture.

### 22. Final conclusion
- **Main job:** State what it means to complete Marx’s theory.
- **Main claim:** Marx’s crisis theory should be completed by developing the excess-capital line together with credit theory, not by preserving excess-commodity explanations.
- **Why it matters:** Gives the paper its final intervention in Marxist crisis theory.
