


# Overview of Arrighi’s paper

Giovanni Arrighi’s **“Towards a Theory of Capitalist Crisis”** argues that capitalist crises are not accidents or policy mistakes. They are built into capitalism itself because accumulation depends on raising productivity while also extracting surplus from workers, and those two pressures repeatedly collide.

A central distinction in the paper is between two crisis tendencies. One is a **realization/overproduction crisis**: exploitation is pushed so high that workers’ consumption is too restricted for commodities to be sold profitably. The other is a **profitability crisis**: exploitation does not rise enough to offset the growing capital intensity of production, so the rate of profit falls. In both cases crisis appears as falling profitability, but the social burden falls differently depending on which mechanism dominates. fileciteturn0file0

Arrighi’s key move is to link the form of crisis to the **balance of forces between capital and labor**. In more competitive capitalism, inter-capitalist rivalry can blunt direct attacks on workers and make crisis show up more as falling profits. As capital becomes concentrated and centralized, firms gain more power to defend prices, cut production, and shift crisis costs onto labor. But over the long run, that same development also concentrates workers, increases their collective power, and can make it harder for capital to restore profitability by raising exploitation. fileciteturn0file0

He then argues that **state intervention cannot abolish crisis**. The state can only manage capitalism’s contradictions temporarily, for example by expanding unproductive consumption, military spending, bureaucracy, or other supports that help realize surplus-value without directly strengthening workers too much. Postwar state management, in his view, did not solve crisis; it shifted its dominant form toward stagnation plus inflation and a falling profit rate rather than the catastrophic overproduction pattern associated with the 1930s. fileciteturn0file0

The paper also expands from national economies to the **world market**. Falling profitability pushes capital to decentralize production toward regions where labor is cheaper and weaker. Arrighi treats this as a structural response to crisis, one that can raise profits while also enlarging the world proletariat. Unlike the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, he argues that the contemporary period is less likely to culminate in intensified protectionism and inter-imperialist war, and more likely to deepen global restructuring and class conflict across regions. fileciteturn0file0

In the final sections, Arrighi examines how crisis splits the bourgeoisie into **advanced** and **backward** fractions. Advanced capital tends to prefer inflationary, restructuring-oriented, internationally mobile strategies; backward capital tends to prefer protection, deflation, repression, and national-state defenses. The political question becomes which fraction can hegemonize intermediate strata and parts of the working class. His broader conclusion is that modern capitalist crisis is increasingly tied to the structural strength of labor, even if capital keeps trying to reorganize production and politics to contain that strength. fileciteturn0file0

The **1978 postscript** qualifies the earlier 1972 argument. Arrighi says the main diagnosis still holds, especially the emphasis on stagflation, persistent profit problems, and intensified market competition rather than a simple replay of the 1930s. But he also admits the original essay treated the strengthening of the working class too schematically and too uniformly, and that future analysis needs to be more differentiated by region, labor composition, and historical form of accumulation. fileciteturn0file0

# Reverse outline

## 1. Opening thesis: crisis is intrinsic to capitalism
- **Main job of the section:** Reject explanations that blame crises on worker militancy, bad management, or social “parasitism.”
- **Main claim:** Crisis arises from the contradiction between capital accumulation and the social development of production.
- **Why it matters:** Sets up crisis as structural, not accidental. fileciteturn0file0

## 2. Two basic mechanisms of crisis
- **Main job of the section:** Define the two ways crisis can emerge.
- **Main claim:** Crisis can come from exploitation being too high to realize surplus-value, or too low to maintain profitability on an expanding capital base.
- **Why it matters:** Establishes the paper’s core analytical framework. fileciteturn0file0

## 3. Distributional consequences of each crisis type
- **Main job of the section:** Show that the two crisis forms are not politically equivalent.
- **Main claim:** When realization fails, workers tend to bear the burden; when profitability falls because exploitation cannot rise enough, capital and dependent strata bear more of the burden.
- **Why it matters:** Moves the discussion from abstract economics to class outcomes. fileciteturn0file0

## 4. Capital concentration and competitive capitalism
- **Main job of the section:** Explain how market structure shapes crisis dynamics.
- **Main claim:** Under competitive capitalism, firms cannot easily defend profits through output restriction, so crisis tends to show up as falling prices and profits rather than sharp attacks on labor.
- **Why it matters:** Provides the historical baseline for later comparison. fileciteturn0file0

## 5. Crisis as a motor of concentration and monopoly
- **Main job of the section:** Show how crisis reorganizes capitalism itself.
- **Main claim:** Crisis eliminates weaker firms, accelerates mergers and centralization, and helps produce monopoly capitalism.
- **Why it matters:** Explains how one crisis reshapes the terrain of the next one. fileciteturn0file0

## 6. Monopoly capitalism and the 1930s pattern
- **Main job of the section:** Contrast the new structure with the earlier competitive phase.
- **Main claim:** Under monopoly conditions, firms can defend prices by cutting production, which raises unemployment and shifts crisis costs more directly onto workers.
- **Why it matters:** Explains why the 1930s looked different from the late nineteenth-century crisis. fileciteturn0file0

## 7. The contradictory effects of accumulation on labor
- **Main job of the section:** Show that accumulation weakens and strengthens labor at the same time.
- **Main claim:** Capitalist development destroys workers’ individual bargaining power but builds their collective power through concentration, homogenization, and socialization of labor.
- **Why it matters:** This is the hinge of Arrighi’s overall theory. fileciteturn0file0

## 8. Historical lag in labor’s strengthening
- **Main job of the section:** Explain why labor’s collective power did not immediately counter monopoly capital.
- **Main claim:** In the early twentieth century, concentration of ownership outpaced concentration of production, and nationalism/imperialism blunted labor antagonism.
- **Why it matters:** Prevents the theory from becoming mechanically deterministic. fileciteturn0file0

## 9. State intervention as crisis management, not solution
- **Main job of the section:** Critique bourgeois theories that treat the state as able to solve crisis.
- **Main claim:** The state can only mediate contradictions, mainly by managing demand and redistributing surplus-value in limited ways.
- **Why it matters:** Reframes the welfare/Keynesian state as a temporary stabilizer, not a cure. fileciteturn0file0

## 10. Unproductive consumption and postwar stabilization
- **Main job of the section:** Explain how states supported accumulation after the 1930s.
- **Main claim:** Military spending, bureaucracy, and other unproductive expenditures helped realize surplus-value without directly redistributing power to labor.
- **Why it matters:** Clarifies Arrighi’s account of the postwar boom. fileciteturn0file0

## 11. The present crisis as stagflation and falling profits
- **Main job of the section:** Distinguish the contemporary crisis from earlier ones.
- **Main claim:** The new crisis resembles the late nineteenth century more than the 1930s because it stems from obstacles to raising exploitation, but now those obstacles come from labor’s collective strength.
- **Why it matters:** States the paper’s main historical diagnosis. fileciteturn0file0

## 12. World market adjustment and international decentralization
- **Main job of the section:** Extend the argument to global capitalism.
- **Main claim:** Falling profits drive capital to shift production to regions with cheaper, weaker labor and to use global trade to restore profitability.
- **Why it matters:** Connects crisis theory to uneven development and global restructuring. fileciteturn0file0

## 13. Why decentralization no longer reproduces the old pattern
- **Main job of the section:** Revisit late nineteenth-century underdevelopment.
- **Main claim:** Earlier decentralization often stalled because incomplete proletarianization, weak labor supplies, colonial relations, and overproduction pushed capital back toward centralization.
- **Why it matters:** Builds the contrast with the postwar era. fileciteturn0file0

## 14. Colonialism, protectionism, and war
- **Main job of the section:** Explain the political forms crisis took in the earlier era.
- **Main claim:** Protectionism and colonialism were tools for displacing crisis costs and restructuring competition through states, often culminating in war.
- **Why it matters:** Shows why the first half of the twentieth century followed a different path. fileciteturn0file0

## 15. Why the contemporary path is different
- **Main job of the section:** Argue that capital now faces a different strategic horizon.
- **Main claim:** Because labor is structurally stronger and primitive accumulation is more complete, capital is pushed toward further world-market integration and decentralization, not primarily toward renewed imperial closure.
- **Why it matters:** This is the paper’s biggest historical prediction. fileciteturn0file0

## 16. Economic crisis becomes political crisis within capital
- **Main job of the section:** Shift from macroeconomics to class fractions and political struggle.
- **Main claim:** Falling profits sharpen conflict between advanced and backward sectors of capital over how the state should be used.
- **Why it matters:** Opens the paper’s final political analysis. fileciteturn0file0

## 17. Advanced vs. backward capital
- **Main job of the section:** Define the two bourgeois strategies.
- **Main claim:** Backward capital favors protection, national-state defense, deflation, and repression; advanced capital favors restructuring, inflationary management, decentralization, and supranational forms.
- **Why it matters:** Supplies the political map of capitalist responses to crisis. fileciteturn0file0

## 18. Competing strategies toward labor
- **Main job of the section:** Show how each fraction of capital tries to manage working-class power.
- **Main claim:** Backward capital prefers frontal attacks; advanced capital prefers partial concessions, co-management, and selective repression.
- **Why it matters:** Explains why capital’s internal divisions matter for workers’ immediate conditions. fileciteturn0file0

## 19. The struggle for hegemony over workers and intermediate strata
- **Main job of the section:** Explain the broader social alliances around each capitalist faction.
- **Main claim:** Each fraction seeks support from middle strata and different segments of labor, especially the reserve army and active workers.
- **Why it matters:** Shows crisis as a struggle over political leadership, not just economics. fileciteturn0file0

## 20. Limits of advanced capital’s “flexibility”
- **Main job of the section:** Undercut any idea that advanced capital is genuinely pro-worker.
- **Main claim:** Inflation, restructuring, and repression reveal that advanced capital’s concessions are tactical and reversible.
- **Why it matters:** Brings the paper back to irreconcilable class antagonism. fileciteturn0file0

## 21. Postscript: reaffirmation with revisions
- **Main job of the section:** Reassess the 1972 argument in light of the mid-1970s.
- **Main claim:** Arrighi maintains that the crisis is centered on falling profitability and stagflation, but admits he treated labor’s strengthening too uniformly and needs a more regionally differentiated framework.
- **Why it matters:** Shows both the durability and the limits of the original argument. fileciteturn0file0



# Intro 

The history of capitalism shows us that the periodic recurrence of crises is not a function of the working class’s strength or combativity, of ‘mistakes’ in economic management, or even of ‘parasitism’ in society.* The tendency towards crisis is indissolubly linked to the existence of capitalism itself. It is a result of the contradiction between the goal of capitalist accumulation (the valorization of capital and the appropriation of surplus-value by capital) and the means by which this goal is pursued (growth in social productivity and the development of the social character of production). Social productivity is increased continuously by mechanization and the division and reorganization of labour, not in order to satisfy the needs of the producers, but in order to increase the proportion of the social product which accrues to capital instead of being passed on to the producers. This process has a contradictory effect on society’s ability to consume and produce. Whilst production (whose growth depends principally on the proportion of the social product which goes to the capitalists and is transformed into means of production) tends to increase, consumption (whose growth depends principally on the proportion of the social product which goes to the workers and which is transformed into means of consumption) tends to contract.

# Crisis and Capital Accumulation 

Commodities produced using the means of production in which capital has been invested are thus always in danger of remaining unsold because of the restricted base of consumption under capitalism. From this spring what are called realization crises. The surplus-value which labour produces and incorporates in commodities is not realized—in other words, it does not form profit—because part of the commodities in question either remain unsold or can only be sold at such low prices that potential profit is reduced or nullified. In this case, the crisis occurs because the rate of exploitation (the relation between the portion of social product which is appropriated by capital and the portion retained by the workers) is ‘too high’ to allow the realization of surplus-value.

If for any reason, on the other hand, the rate of exploitation does not rise and stays constant (or even falls), accumulation no longer tends to run up against over-restricted consumption, since workers’ incomes rise in step with productivity. In this case, accumulation runs up against the limits set by the fall in the rate of profit (the ratio of profit to invested capital). A constant (or diminishing) proportion of the social product is insufficient to remunerate, at a constant rate, the ever-increasing mass of capital that the capitalists have to invest per unit of product. If exploitation stays constant (or falls), the rate of profit falls with capital intensity in production.1 There is hence a tendency for a reduction in accumulation to take place, because the capitalists do not get the returns they expected from their investment. In this case, the crisis is brought on because the rate of exploitation is ‘too low’ for an ‘adequate’ remuneration of capital.

In both cases the crisis is manifested as a fall in the rate of profit and overproduction of commodities: in the first case (rate of exploitation ‘too high’) the rate of profit falls because there is overproduction of commodities and surplus-value cannot be completely transformed into profits; in the second case (rate of exploitation ‘too low’) there is overproduction because the fall in the rate of profit brings about a diminished demand for means of production.
