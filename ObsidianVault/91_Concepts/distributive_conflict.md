# Distributive Conflict

## Definition
The antagonistic struggle between capital and labour over the division of net output — expressed as the wage share ω (labour) and profit share π (capital). Distributive conflict is not a market residual but the constitutive mechanism through which the accumulation regime reproduces, transforms, or destabilizes itself.

## Formal Expression
`ω + π = 1` (accounting identity at current prices)

But the dynamics of ω are not governed by marginal productivity pricing. They emerge from the balance of class forces: labour-market tightness, union strength, state intervention, and international competition (exchange rate, import pressure on wages).

## Path Dependency
The path of ω feeds back into the transformation elasticity:
`θ(ω) = θ̃₀ − θ₁·ω`

A higher ω drives firms to adopt more capital-intensive techniques (mechanization response to labour cost pressure), which reduces θ toward and below 1. Once below the knife-edge, the structural over-accumulation tendency is self-reinforcing: falling profit rates → reduced investment → slower capacity growth → further competitive pressure → further mechanization → lower θ.

This makes the long-run structural tendency endogenous to the history of class conflict — path dependency through the wage share.

## Fordist Settlement and Its Limits
The Fordist wage compromise indexed wages to productivity — stabilizing ω at a level consistent with mass consumption demand. But full-employment policy eroded the reserve army of labour, tightening labour markets and pushing ω above ω_H (the Harrodian knife-edge). By the late 1960s, the distributional norm that sustained Fordism became the mechanism of its own dissolution.

## Chilean Context
In Chile, distributive conflict takes a distinct form under peripheral accumulation:
- The BoP constraint transmits world-market price shocks into domestic income distribution
- The 1970–1973 Unidad Popular period represents the extreme of distributive conflict: rapid wage share expansion, π = 1.381 in the 1972–74 swing
- The 1973 coup inverts the distributional balance via shock therapy — rapid π recovery through real wage destruction
