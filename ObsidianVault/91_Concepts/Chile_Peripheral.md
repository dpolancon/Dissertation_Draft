# Chile as Peripheral Economy

## Definition

**Chile** in this dissertation is treated as a **peripheral economy** in the world-system sense: a late-industrializing, primary-commodity-exporting economy whose accumulation dynamics are structurally conditioned by two external constraints:

1. **Capital composition constraint:** Domestic capital formation depends on imported machinery and equipment (capital goods from the center). This creates a technological dependency that modifies the mechanization possibility frontier — introducing the BoP term φ into θ(ω, φ).

2. **Balance-of-payments constraint:** The external account (current + capital) governs the availability of foreign exchange, which in turn governs the leverage ratio (LevR_t). When LevR falls below the legal minimum, the external constraint binds and domestic policy space collapses.

## Two appearances in the dissertation

### Chapter 2 — Peripheral extension of the MPF

Chile is the **peripheral case study** for the structural identification of θ. The US identification (Stage A) treats θ as a function of the wage share ω only. The Chilean extension adds the BoP term:

`θ(ω, φ) = θ̃₀ + θ₃·φ + θ₂·ω·φ`

The identification requires a bivariate cointegrating system (two CEs: capacity frontier CV1 and BoP equilibrium CV2). See [[eq_cl_theta]] and [[ch2_S4_Chile]].

### Chapter 3 — Political Economy of the Unidad Popular

Chile's leverage ratio trajectory under the UP (1970–73) is the empirical referent for the theoretical formalization of the BoP constraint. The leverage ratio law of motion ([[eq_levr_lom]]) is tested against three historical scenarios:
- (A) pre-UP fiscal monetization
- (B) UP 1970–73
- (C) post-1973 shock therapy

## Structural difference from US NFC

The US NFC sector faces no binding BoP constraint in the Fordist sample. The Chilean peripheral economy does — the BoP term φ is not merely a control variable but identifies a structurally different regime of accumulation. This is the theoretical contribution of the peripheral extension.
