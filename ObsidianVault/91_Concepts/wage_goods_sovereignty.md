# Wage-Goods Sovereignty

## Definition
The productive capacity to domestically supply the wage-goods basket — the consumption goods required for social reproduction of the working class — at scale and at competitive cost. An economy possesses wage-goods sovereignty when rising real wages translate into increased domestic demand for domestically produced goods, rather than into import demand denominated in foreign currency.

## Why It Matters
Wage-goods sovereignty is the structural condition that determines whether distributive conflict carries a financial fragility dimension:

| Condition | Wage share rises → | BoP effect | Accumulation regime |
|-----------|-------------------|------------|-------------------|
| **With** sovereignty (center) | Domestic demand expansion → domestic production absorbs | Neutral or positive (multiplier) | Profit squeeze only — standard Goodwin |
| **Without** sovereignty (periphery) | Import demand expansion → reserve drainage | Negative (BoP deterioration) | Profit squeeze + financial fragility — double channel |

This is the structural distinction the cosmic historian identified as Discovery 3: distributive conflict at the periphery has a financial fragility dimension the center does not share, because the center's wage goods are domestically produced.

## Mechanism: How Absence Activates Financial Fragility
In an economy without wage-goods sovereignty:

1. **Rising ω** → higher real wages → increased demand for consumption goods
2. **Import dependency** → wage-goods basket includes imported food, consumer durables, intermediate inputs for domestic consumer goods production
3. **Reserve drainage** → imports rise, denominated in reserve currency (USD) → IR falls → LevR deteriorates
4. **BoP binding** → when LevR falls below threshold, the external constraint activates all four inflation channels simultaneously (exchange rate, monetary, imported inflation, supply constraint)

The result: rising wage share simultaneously compresses profits (standard channel) AND drains reserves (financial fragility channel). The two effects compound — this is why ζ̂₃ = −3.919 is a single coefficient concealing a two-dimensional structural process.

## Chile's Specific Position
Chile lacked wage-goods sovereignty across the entire period studied in Ch3:
- **Food imports**: despite agrarian reform, food self-sufficiency was never achieved; wheat imports persisted
- **Consumer durables**: dependent on imported intermediate inputs and capital goods for domestic assembly
- **Capital goods for consumer-goods production**: the mechanization possibility frontier ([[mechanization_possibility_frontier]]) was BoP-constrained — producing wage goods domestically required importing the capital goods to produce them

This created a structural trap: expanding domestic production to achieve wage-goods sovereignty required the very imports that the absence of sovereignty made unsustainable. The leverage ratio formalizes this trap as a law of motion.

## Counterfactual: What Sovereignty Would Change
If Chile had possessed wage-goods sovereignty:
- ζ̂₃ would carry only the profit-squeeze channel (smaller magnitude, standard Goodwin dynamics)
- Rising ω under the UP would have expanded domestic demand without proportional reserve drainage
- The LevR deterioration path would have been slower or absent
- The double squeeze (Chains P + Q in the causal graph) would not have operated

This counterfactual is not hypothetical — it describes the US case in Ch2, where wage-goods sovereignty was present and ζ̂₃'s financial fragility channel was structurally suppressed.
